# Multi-Residential/Apartments Guide

## Building Classification for Multi-Residential Projects

### Class 2 — Apartment Buildings

A Class 2 building is a building containing two or more sole-occupancy units (SOUs), each being a separate dwelling. This is the primary classification for apartment buildings, residential flat buildings, and multi-storey unit complexes in Australia. The NCC defines a sole-occupancy unit as a room or suite of rooms for exclusive use by the occupants, typically comprising a kitchen, living area, bedroom(s), and bathroom. Each SOU must be self-contained — it must have its own kitchen, bathroom, and independent entry.

The distinction between Class 2 and other residential classifications is critical. A building with two or more attached dwellings separated by a fire wall (terraces, row houses, townhouses) is classified as Class 1a, not Class 2. The key determinant is whether the dwellings are "within a building" (Class 2) or are separate buildings attached by a fire wall (Class 1a). In practical terms, if the dwellings share a common structural frame, common corridors, or common vertical circulation (stairs, lifts), the building is almost certainly Class 2. This classification triggers NCC Volume 1 provisions, which are significantly more onerous than the Volume 2 provisions that apply to Class 1.

Class 2 buildings are governed by NCC Volume 1, which imposes requirements for fire resistance, compartmentation, egress, accessibility, services, and amenity that are substantially more demanding than those for detached houses. The rise in storeys determines the Type of Construction required (A, B, or C), which in turn sets the fire resistance levels for all structural and separating elements.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1 Part A6 — Building Classifications (Clause A6.0 to A6.5)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all multi-residential project types
Related standards: State building legislation — Environmental Planning and Assessment Act 1979 (NSW), Building Act 1993 (VIC), Building Act 2004 (QLD), Building Act 2011 (WA)

Common pitfalls:
- Misclassifying attached townhouses or terraces as Class 2 when they are Class 1a — the distinction affects which NCC volume applies and the applicable fire safety, accessibility, and energy efficiency provisions
- Assuming a small two-storey block of four units can use the simpler Volume 2 provisions because it "looks like" a house — any building with two or more SOUs within a single building structure is Class 2 regardless of size
- Not recognising that a building with serviced apartments or short-stay accommodation may be Class 3 rather than Class 2 if the occupants do not have exclusive use of the unit (e.g., hotel-style cleaning and management services are provided)

### Class 3 — Residential Buildings (Transient/Communal)

Class 3 buildings are residential buildings other than Class 1 or 2 that provide a common place of long-term or transient living for unrelated persons. This includes boarding houses exceeding the Class 1b thresholds (more than 12 persons or more than 300 m2), hotels, motels, backpacker hostels, residential parts of schools, aged care buildings (except those classified as Class 9c), and worker accommodation. The defining characteristic is communal or managed living arrangements rather than fully independent self-contained dwellings.

Class 3 buildings have more onerous NCC requirements than Class 2 in several areas, reflecting the higher vulnerability of occupants who may be unfamiliar with the building layout or have limited capacity for self-evacuation. Fire safety requirements, including detection, alarm systems, and egress provisions, are more stringent. The distinction between Class 2 and Class 3 can be particularly nuanced for serviced apartments, student accommodation, and aged care independent living units — the determining factor is the nature of the occupancy arrangement rather than the physical layout.

For project managers and quantity surveyors, the financial impact of a Class 3 classification versus Class 2 is significant. Class 3 buildings typically require more extensive fire detection and alarm systems (often a fully addressable system rather than standalone smoke alarms), more demanding egress provisions, and may trigger additional accessibility and amenity requirements. A reclassification from Class 2 to Class 3 during the approval process can add substantial cost.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1 Part A6 — Building Classifications; ABCB advisory notes on building classification
Applies to: Class 3 buildings; boarding houses, hotels, student accommodation, aged care
Related standards: Boarding Houses Act 2012 (NSW); Residential Tenancies Act (various states); aged care facility design standards

Common pitfalls:
- Classifying student accommodation as Class 2 when the management model (cleaning, food service, room assignment) makes it Class 3
- Not considering that a building's classification may change if the occupancy model changes — converting a Class 2 apartment building to short-stay accommodation may trigger a reclassification to Class 3
- Failing to account for the additional cost and compliance requirements of Class 3 classification during feasibility assessment

### Class 4 — Dwelling Within Another Classification

A Class 4 part is a dwelling or residence located within a building of a different classification — the most common example being a caretaker's flat above a shop (Class 6) or within an office building (Class 5). The Class 4 part must comply with certain provisions of NCC Volume 2 (the residential volume) even though it is located within a Volume 1 building. This creates a dual-compliance requirement that is frequently overlooked.

The Class 4 part must be separated from the remainder of the building by fire-resisting construction with an FRL determined by the Type of Construction of the building. It must have independent egress that does not pass through the non-residential part of the building. The amenity, services, and energy efficiency provisions applicable to the Class 4 part are drawn from Volume 2 rather than Volume 1, which can create coordination challenges when detailing wall assemblies, insulation, and services connections.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1 Part A6.6 (Class 4 parts of buildings); Volume 2 applicability for Class 4
Applies to: Mixed-use buildings with residential caretaker or manager accommodation
Related standards: Planning instruments that require or permit caretaker accommodation in commercial or industrial zones

Common pitfalls:
- Not identifying the Class 4 part during the design phase, leading to non-compliant fire separation and egress arrangements discovered during certification
- Applying Volume 1 amenity provisions to the Class 4 dwelling instead of the Volume 2 provisions that actually apply
- Not providing independent egress from the Class 4 part, particularly in industrial or warehouse buildings where the only access is through the Class 8 workspace

## Fire Safety in Multi-Residential Buildings

### Type of Construction and Fire Resistance Levels

Fire safety is the most consequential and complex compliance area for multi-residential buildings. The fire resistance requirements are determined by the building's Type of Construction, which in turn depends on the building classification and rise in storeys. For Class 2 buildings, the requirements escalate significantly as the building height increases.

Type C construction (the least fire resistant) applies to single-storey Class 2 buildings. Type B construction applies to Class 2 buildings with a rise in storeys of 2 or 3. Type A construction (the most fire resistant) applies to Class 2 buildings with a rise in storeys of 4 or more. For Type A construction, primary structural elements typically require an FRL of 90/90/90 or 120/120/120 depending on the storey position within the building. Type B construction requires FRLs of 60/60/60 for most elements.

The distinction between Type A and Type B is critical for project cost. Type A construction requires fire-rated shafts, fire-rated corridors, higher-grade fire doors, and more extensive passive fire protection to structural steelwork — all of which add significant cost. For a four-storey apartment building sitting exactly at the Type A threshold, a Performance Solution that permits Type B construction through the provision of a compliant sprinkler system can deliver substantial savings. However, this creates a permanent dependency on the sprinkler system.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Section C Part C1 (Fire resistance and stability), Specification C1.2 (Table C1.2 — Type of construction)
Applies to: Class 2, 3 buildings; all multi-residential project types
Related standards: AS 1530.4 (Fire resistance tests of elements of building construction)

Common pitfalls:
- Miscounting the rise in storeys by excluding mezzanines or plant rooms that meet the NCC definition of a "storey"
- Not coordinating fire resistance requirements with the structural engineer early in design, leading to late-stage changes to member sizes, fire protection systems, or both
- Assuming that a sprinkler concession automatically applies without confirming the sprinkler system meets the specific NCC specification (AS 2118.1 to Specification 17)

### SOU Separation — Fire and Acoustic

The separation between sole-occupancy units (SOUs) is governed by two distinct but overlapping NCC requirements: fire separation (Part C2.7) and acoustic separation (Part F5). Wall and floor assemblies between SOUs must satisfy both requirements simultaneously, which often drives the selection of construction systems.

Fire separation between SOUs requires fire-resisting construction with an FRL determined by the Type of Construction. For Type B construction, walls between SOUs typically require FRL 60/60/60. For Type A construction, this increases to FRL 90/90/90 in certain configurations. Floors between SOUs have similar FRL requirements. These fire-rated assemblies must be continuous from slab to slab (or roof) and must not be compromised by service penetrations, junction details, or construction tolerances.

Acoustic separation between SOUs is governed by NCC Volume 1, Part F5 (Sound insulation). The performance requirements are expressed as weighted sound reduction indices. For walls between SOUs, the DTS requirement is Rw + Ctr >= 50 (airborne sound). For floors between SOUs, the requirements address both airborne sound (Rw + Ctr >= 50) and impact sound (Ln,w + CI <= 62 for floors separating habitable rooms from habitable rooms below). In practice, achieving the impact sound requirement is often more challenging than the airborne requirement and frequently drives the floor construction system selection.

The cost implication is that inter-tenancy walls and floors in apartment buildings are among the most expensive elements per square metre because they must achieve fire, acoustic, structural, and waterproofing performance simultaneously. Common construction systems include:
- Concrete block walls with fire-rated and acoustic-rated finishes
- Double-stud timber or steel framed walls with multiple layers of plasterboard and acoustic insulation
- Concrete slab floors with acoustic underlays and floating screeds for impact sound control

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part C2.7 (Separation of sole-occupancy units), Part F5 (Sound insulation)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment projects
Related standards: AS 1530.4 (Fire resistance tests); AS/NZS ISO 717.1 (Rating of sound insulation — airborne); AS/NZS ISO 717.2 (Rating of sound insulation — impact)

Common pitfalls:
- Selecting a wall system that achieves the fire FRL but fails the acoustic Rw + Ctr requirement, or vice versa — both must be satisfied
- Not testing the as-built acoustic performance, which frequently falls short of the designed performance due to construction quality issues (gaps, missing insulation, flanking transmission through junctions)
- Failing to protect service penetrations through fire-rated inter-tenancy walls, which is the single most common fire safety defect in Australian apartment buildings
- Not addressing impact sound transmission through floors, which generates more occupant complaints than airborne sound in completed buildings

### Fire Detection and Alarm Systems

Multi-residential buildings require fire detection and alarm systems that meet the requirements of NCC Volume 1, Part E1 (now Section E under NCC 2022 restructure). The type and extent of the system depends on the building's classification, rise in storeys, and whether sprinklers are installed.

For Class 2 buildings, the minimum DTS requirement is smoke alarms complying with AS 3786 in each SOU, installed in accordance with Part E2.2a (now Part E7 under NCC 2022). Smoke alarms must be installed in every bedroom, in every hallway or corridor associated with a bedroom, and on every storey of the SOU. In buildings with a rise in storeys of two or more, the smoke alarms must be interconnected so that activation of any alarm triggers all alarms in the SOU.

For Class 2 buildings with a rise in storeys of 4 or more, additional requirements apply. An automatic fire detection and alarm system to AS 1670.1 is typically required in common areas (corridors, lobbies, plant rooms, car parks). The fire indicator panel (FIP) must be located at the main entrance to the building in a position accessible to the fire brigade.

For Class 3 buildings (which are subject to more onerous requirements), a fully addressable fire detection and alarm system to AS 1670.1 is typically required throughout the building, including within each room. This is one of the most significant cost differences between Class 2 and Class 3.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Section E (Services and equipment), Part E1/E2/E7 (Fire detection and alarm); AS 1670.1 (Fire detection, warning, control and intercom systems)
Applies to: Class 2, 3 buildings; all multi-residential project types
Related standards: AS 3786 (Smoke alarms); AS 1670.1 (Fire detection and alarm systems); state-specific smoke alarm legislation

Common pitfalls:
- Not meeting state-specific smoke alarm requirements that exceed the NCC minimum — QLD (from 2027) requires interconnected photoelectric smoke alarms in all existing dwellings, and several states require hardwired alarms in new buildings
- Failing to interconnect smoke alarms within each SOU in buildings with a rise in storeys of two or more
- Not providing a fire indicator panel at the main entrance for buildings that require an AS 1670.1 system
- Omitting fire detection in car parks, plant rooms, and garbage rooms in the common areas

### Sprinkler Systems in Apartment Buildings

Automatic fire sprinkler systems are required in Class 2 buildings with an effective height of more than 25 m under the DTS provisions of NCC Volume 1, Part E1.5 (Specification 17). Buildings below this threshold may still install sprinklers voluntarily to access sprinkler concessions for Type of Construction, compartment sizes, and travel distances.

The relevant sprinkler standard depends on the building height. For Class 2 buildings up to 4 storeys, AS 2118.4 (residential sprinkler systems) provides a simplified and lower-cost alternative to the general standard. For buildings above 4 storeys or with an effective height exceeding 25 m, AS 2118.1 (general fire sprinkler systems) applies. The distinction matters because AS 2118.4 systems use smaller pipe sizes, lower water pressures, and simpler hydraulic calculations, resulting in substantially lower installation costs.

For projects where the building is just below the mandatory sprinkler threshold, a cost-benefit analysis should be conducted comparing the sprinkler installation cost against the savings from sprinkler concessions. In many cases, voluntarily installing sprinklers in a 3-4 storey Class 2 building delivers a net cost saving because the reduction in fire-rated construction, increased compartment sizes, and extended travel distances more than offset the sprinkler system cost.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part E1.5 (Sprinkler systems), Specification 17; AS 2118.1 (General sprinkler systems), AS 2118.4 (Residential sprinkler systems)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: AS 2941 (Fire pumpset systems); AS 2419.1 (Fire hydrant installations)

Common pitfalls:
- Assuming the simpler AS 2118.4 residential sprinkler standard applies to a building that exceeds 4 storeys, requiring the more complex and expensive AS 2118.1 general standard
- Not conducting an early cost-benefit analysis of voluntary sprinkler installation for buildings just below the mandatory threshold
- Failing to coordinate sprinkler pipe routing with ceiling linings and other services in apartments, leading to exposed pipes or insufficient ceiling space
- Not providing adequate water supply (tank and pump) for the sprinkler system, particularly where town mains pressure is insufficient

### Egress from Multi-Residential Buildings

Egress design in Class 2 buildings must comply with NCC Volume 1, Section D (Access and egress). The egress requirements address travel distances within SOUs, travel distances in common corridors, the number of exits from each storey, and the construction and protection of exit stairs.

Within each SOU, the maximum travel distance from any point to the entrance door of the SOU is typically 6 m. This intra-unit travel distance is often overlooked but can constrain apartment layouts, particularly large penthouse units or units with unusual shapes. From the SOU entrance door to the nearest required exit (fire stair), the maximum travel distance in a common corridor is 20 m (or 40 m to the nearest of two or more exits). These distances increase for sprinklered buildings.

For buildings with a rise in storeys of two or more, fire-isolated stairs (fire stairs) are required. Fire-isolated stairs must be enclosed by fire-resisting construction with an FRL matching the Type of Construction, have self-closing fire doors, and discharge directly at ground level to a road or open space. The stair width must accommodate the occupant load, with a minimum clear width of 1 m. For buildings of 8 or more storeys, fire-isolated stairs must also have stair pressurisation systems to prevent smoke entering the stair enclosure.

The number of exits required from each storey depends on the occupant load. Most residential floors with a moderate number of apartments can be served by two exits located to provide alternative paths of travel. Single-exit configurations (dead-end corridors) are permitted only for very small floor plates with limited travel distances.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Section D (Access and egress), Part D1 (Provision for escape), Part D2 (Construction of exits)
Applies to: Class 2, 3 buildings; all multi-residential project types
Related standards: AS 1428.1 (Design for access and mobility); AS/NZS 2293.1 (Emergency escape lighting)

Common pitfalls:
- Exceeding the 6 m intra-unit travel distance in large apartments or irregular floor plans, which is a compliance issue that is difficult to resolve without changing the apartment layout
- Designing a single fire stair for a building that requires two exits based on the occupant load or travel distance
- Not providing stair pressurisation for buildings of 8 or more storeys, which is frequently omitted from early services budgets
- Failing to ensure fire stairs discharge directly to a road or open space at ground level — stairs that discharge into a foyer or car park may not comply

## Accessibility and Universal Design

### NCC Accessibility Requirements for Class 2

Accessibility provisions for Class 2 buildings are addressed in NCC Volume 1, Section D (Access and egress) and the Premises Standards (Disability Discrimination Act 1992 — Disability (Access to Premises — Buildings) Standards 2010). These provisions apply to the common areas of the building and, in certain jurisdictions, to a proportion of the sole-occupancy units.

The NCC requires that common areas in Class 2 buildings provide accessible paths of travel from the main entrance to each SOU entrance, including accessible routes through lobbies, corridors, and via at least one accessible lift (for buildings with a rise in storeys of two or more). The accessible path must comply with AS 1428.1, which specifies minimum corridor widths (1,000 mm clear, 1,200 mm preferred), passing spaces, door widths (minimum 850 mm clear), threshold heights (maximum 5 mm), and ramp gradients (maximum 1:14 for short ramps, 1:20 for longer ramps).

Lifts in Class 2 buildings must be accessible, with at least one lift in each bank complying with AS 1735.12 (lift facilities for persons with disabilities). This requires minimum internal dimensions of 1,100 mm wide x 1,400 mm deep, doors opening to a minimum of 900 mm, Braille and tactile numbering on controls, and audible floor annunciation.

The Premises Standards (DDA) require that at least one main entrance to the building and the path of travel from the entrance to each SOU entrance be accessible. Common facilities (mail rooms, garbage areas, communal spaces, car parks) must also be accessible. The interplay between the NCC and the Premises Standards is important — the Premises Standards prevail to the extent of any inconsistency, but in practice the NCC DTS provisions for accessibility are drafted to achieve compliance with both frameworks.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Section D (Access and egress), Part D3 (Access for people with a disability); Disability (Access to Premises — Buildings) Standards 2010
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: AS 1428.1 (Design for access and mobility — general requirements); AS 1735.12 (Lift facilities for persons with disabilities)

Common pitfalls:
- Providing an accessible path from the entrance to the lift but not from the lift to the SOU entrance on each level (the entire path must be continuous)
- Not meeting the minimum lift car dimensions required by AS 1735.12, particularly in buildings where the lift shaft was sized based on standard commercial lift dimensions
- Overlooking the requirement for accessible car parking spaces in the basement (typically a proportion of total spaces)
- Not complying with the luminance contrast requirements of AS 1428.1 for doors, walls, and fittings along the accessible path

### Adaptable Housing and Livable Housing Design Guidelines

Beyond the mandatory NCC and Premises Standards requirements, several Australian jurisdictions impose adaptable housing or universal design requirements on apartment developments through planning controls. The most significant national framework is the Livable Housing Design Guidelines (LHDG), published by Livable Housing Australia, which establishes three performance levels: Silver, Gold, and Platinum.

The NCC 2022 introduced new accessibility provisions for SOUs in Class 2 buildings. The key change is that a proportion of apartments in new Class 2 buildings must now meet minimum accessibility criteria within the SOU itself, not just in the common areas. These provisions include:
- A step-free entrance to the SOU (or a step-free path from the accessible common area to the SOU entrance)
- A toilet on the entry level of the SOU accessible to a wheelchair user
- Reinforced bathroom walls to allow future installation of grab rails
- Minimum doorway widths within the SOU (820 mm clear for internal doors)
- Minimum corridor widths within the SOU (1,000 mm clear)

State planning requirements vary significantly. NSW SEPP 65 (Design Quality of Residential Apartment Development) and the Apartment Design Guide (ADG) impose additional requirements for apartment dimensions, natural ventilation, solar access, and common area amenity. Victoria's Better Apartments Design Standards apply to developments of five or more dwellings. Queensland's State Planning Policy includes accessibility requirements for multi-residential developments.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part D4 (Access within sole-occupancy units in Class 2 buildings); Livable Housing Design Guidelines (Silver, Gold, Platinum levels)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; new apartment developments
Related standards: AS 4299 (Adaptable housing); Apartment Design Guides (state-specific: NSW ADG, VIC Better Apartments)

Common pitfalls:
- Not including the cost of NCC 2022 SOU accessibility provisions in the feasibility estimate, as these provisions apply to all new Class 2 buildings regardless of planning requirements
- Designing apartments to the minimum NCC corridor and door widths without checking whether the applicable state planning controls require larger dimensions
- Treating adaptable housing as an afterthought rather than integrating it into the initial apartment layout design, which avoids costly redesign
- Not reinforcing bathroom walls for future grab rail installation during the rough-in phase, which is extremely difficult and expensive to retrofit

## Waterproofing in Multi-Residential Buildings

### Wet Area Waterproofing

Waterproofing is the single largest source of defect claims in Australian apartment buildings. The Building Confidence Report (Shergold-Weir, 2018) identified waterproofing as one of the most common categories of building defects, and subsequent studies across multiple states have consistently confirmed that wet area waterproofing failures are the predominant defect type in Class 2 buildings.

NCC Volume 1, Part F1.7 (Waterproofing of wet areas) references AS 3740 (Waterproofing of domestic wet areas) as the primary DTS standard for internal wet areas. AS 3740 specifies the minimum requirements for waterproofing membranes in bathrooms, laundries, and other areas subject to water spillage. The standard requires:
- Waterproofing membrane applied to the floor of all wet areas, extending up walls to a minimum height of 150 mm (and 1,800 mm in shower areas)
- Waterproofing of the floor and walls of shower recesses to a height of 1,800 mm (or to the ceiling height if the shower is unscreened)
- Membrane continuity at all junctions, corners, penetrations, and floor waste connections
- A fall to floor wastes of minimum 1:80 (1.25%)
- Membrane material and installation to comply with AS 4858 (wet area waterproofing — installation)

In Class 2 buildings, the consequences of waterproofing failure are amplified because water damage from one apartment typically affects the apartments below. A single bathroom leak on a high floor can cascade through multiple levels, causing damage to ceilings, walls, finishes, and the personal property of occupants on every level below. The cost of rectifying a waterproofing failure in an occupied apartment building — which requires vacating the affected apartments, stripping and replacing finishes, and making good — is orders of magnitude higher than the cost of correct installation during construction.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part F1.7 (Waterproofing of wet areas); AS 3740:2021 (Waterproofing of domestic wet areas); AS 4858 (Wet area waterproofing — installation)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: AS 4654.1, .2 (Waterproofing membranes for external above-ground use)

Common pitfalls:
- Not extending the waterproofing membrane to the correct height on walls (1,800 mm in shower areas is the minimum; full height is recommended)
- Inadequate membrane treatment at junctions between the floor and walls, particularly at internal and external corners where membrane cracking is most likely
- Failing to test the waterproofing membrane before tiling, which means defects are only discovered after the tiles are installed and the cost of rectification has multiplied
- Not protecting the waterproofing membrane during subsequent trades (tiling, plumbing fit-off), leading to mechanical damage to the membrane

### Balcony and External Waterproofing

Balcony waterproofing is governed by AS 4654.1 and AS 4654.2 (Waterproofing membranes for external above-ground use) rather than AS 3740, because balconies are exposed to weather and temperature cycling that internal wet areas are not. The performance requirements for external membranes are more demanding because the membrane must withstand UV exposure, thermal expansion and contraction, wind-driven rain, and structural movement.

The most common balcony waterproofing systems in Australian apartment construction are:
- Liquid-applied polyurethane or polyurea membranes over a concrete substrate with falls
- Sheet membrane systems (PVC, TPO, or modified bitumen) bonded or mechanically fixed to the substrate
- Cementitious waterproofing systems with flexible polymer modification for smaller balconies

Critical design considerations for balcony waterproofing include:
- **Minimum falls:** AS 4654.2 requires a minimum fall of 1:60 (1.67%) to drainage outlets. In practice, 1:40 is recommended to allow for construction tolerances and long-term deflection.
- **Upturns at thresholds:** The waterproofing membrane must extend up the wall and behind the door frame to form a continuous upstand. The height of the upstand depends on the exposure category, but a minimum of 150 mm is required in most situations.
- **Drainage:** Balcony drainage must be designed to handle the design rainfall intensity for the location, including adequate floor waste grate sizes and overflow provisions.
- **Structural movement:** Balconies experience more structural deflection than internal floors because they are cantilever or semi-cantilever elements. The waterproofing system must accommodate this movement without cracking.

Reference: AS 4654.1:2012 (Waterproofing membranes for external above-ground use — materials); AS 4654.2:2012 (Design and installation)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment projects with balconies
Related standards: AS 3740 (Wet area waterproofing — for comparison); NCC Volume 1, Part F1.7

Common pitfalls:
- Using an AS 3740 internal wet area membrane system on an external balcony, which is not designed for UV exposure, temperature cycling, or structural movement
- Insufficient falls to drainage outlets, leading to ponding water, accelerated membrane deterioration, and eventual leaking into the apartment below
- Inadequate threshold upstand height, allowing wind-driven rain to penetrate beneath the sliding door threshold and into the apartment
- Not detailing the junction between the balcony waterproofing and the facade system, which is a common failure point
- Omitting overflow drainage provisions, which are required to prevent water accumulating to a depth that exceeds the threshold upstand if the primary drain is blocked

### Roof Waterproofing and Trafficable Roofs

Multi-residential buildings frequently include trafficable roofs for communal outdoor areas, rooftop gardens, or private terraces on penthouse apartments. Trafficable roof waterproofing combines the challenges of external waterproofing with the structural and drainage demands of a usable outdoor space.

The waterproofing system for a trafficable roof must comply with AS 4654.1 and AS 4654.2, plus additional requirements for the traffic loading, surface finish, and drainage system. Common systems include:
- Inverted roof (protected membrane) systems with insulation over the membrane and pavers or decking above
- Conventional systems with the membrane above the insulation, protected by screed and tiles or pavers on pedestals
- Green roof systems with waterproofing membrane, root barrier, drainage layer, growing medium, and planting

Critical design considerations for trafficable roofs include:
- **Drainage:** Multiple drainage points with adequate capacity for the design rainfall intensity, plus overflow scuppers at a height below the lowest door threshold
- **Waterproofing inspection and maintenance:** The membrane must be accessible for inspection and repair. Systems with pavers on pedestals are preferred because the pavers can be lifted to access the membrane. Membrane systems buried under screed and tiles require destructive investigation to locate leaks
- **Fall-protection barriers:** Trafficable roofs require edge protection complying with NCC Part D2 and AS 1170.1 barrier loads. The height of the barrier depends on the building height, with a minimum of 1 m for buildings up to 4 storeys and increasing for taller buildings
- **Wind loading:** Pavers, furniture, and other items on trafficable roofs must be secured against wind uplift. AS/NZS 1170.2 specifies wind pressures for rooftop areas that can significantly exceed those for lower levels

Reference: AS 4654.1, .2 (Waterproofing membranes for external above-ground use); NCC 2022, Volume 1 Part F1.7 (Waterproofing)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings with trafficable roofs; penthouse developments, mixed-use podiums
Related standards: AS/NZS 1170.1 (Structural design actions — permanent, imposed); AS/NZS 1170.2 (Wind actions)

Common pitfalls:
- Burying the waterproofing membrane under fixed finishes (screed and tiles) on a trafficable roof, making leak detection and repair extremely difficult and expensive
- Not providing adequate overflow drainage provisions, which can lead to water accumulation exceeding the door threshold height during extreme rainfall events
- Underestimating the maintenance requirements of trafficable roof waterproofing systems, which require regular inspection and maintenance to remain effective over the building's life

## Facade and Cladding Systems

### Facade Selection for Multi-Residential Buildings

Facade system selection for apartment buildings involves balancing aesthetic intent, NCC compliance, weatherproofing performance, thermal performance, acoustic performance (for units facing roads or railways), cost, and maintenance. The post-Grenfell and post-Lacrosse regulatory environment has significantly tightened the requirements for facade materials, particularly regarding combustibility.

NCC Volume 1, Part C1.9 (non-combustible building elements) requires that external walls of Type A and Type B construction be non-combustible, with specific concessions for certain materials and components. Following the Lacrosse apartment fire (Melbourne, 2014) and the Grenfell Tower fire (London, 2017), the NCC was amended to introduce more prescriptive requirements for external wall cladding systems, particularly regarding aluminium composite panels (ACPs) and expanded polystyrene (EPS) cladding.

The key NCC provisions for facade materials are:
- **Part C1.9** requires external walls to be non-combustible for Type A and Type B construction (the construction types that apply to most apartment buildings of 2 or more storeys)
- **Part C1.9(e)** provides concessions for certain materials attached to a non-combustible external wall, subject to limitations on height, fire separation, and material type
- **Verification Method CV3** provides a pathway for testing facade systems that include combustible components, using the full-scale facade fire test to AS 5113

Common facade systems for Australian apartment buildings include:
- Masonry veneer (brick or stone) on structural frame — traditional and highly fire-resistant
- Precast concrete panels — common for mid-rise to high-rise
- Unitised curtain wall systems — for high-rise tower buildings
- Fibre cement cladding — lightweight, non-combustible
- Metal cladding (solid aluminium or steel) — non-combustible
- Rendered external insulation and finish systems (EIFS) — require careful material selection to avoid combustible insulation components

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part C1.9 (Non-combustible building elements), Verification Method CV3; AS 5113 (Fire propagation testing and classification of external walls)
Applies to: Class 2, 3 buildings; all multi-residential project types
Related standards: AS 1530.1 (Combustibility test for materials); AS 5113 (External wall fire propagation); state-specific cladding rectification schemes

Common pitfalls:
- Specifying aluminium composite panels (ACPs) with a polyethylene core on a multi-storey apartment building — PE-core ACPs are combustible and do not comply with Part C1.9 for Type A or Type B construction
- Not testing or certifying an EIFS (rendered insulation) system against AS 5113 when the insulation component may be combustible
- Failing to maintain adequate records of the facade materials used during construction, which is now a regulatory requirement in most states following cladding rectification audits
- Not considering the long-term maintenance implications of facade selection — some cladding systems require periodic re-sealing, repainting, or replacement at significant cost

### Weatherproofing and Facade Defects

Facade weatherproofing failures are the second most common defect category (after waterproofing) in Australian apartment buildings. The most frequent failure modes are water ingress at window-to-wall junctions, balcony-to-facade junctions, expansion joints, and penetrations through the external wall for services (exhaust fans, air conditioning pipes, cable entries).

Facade weatherproofing relies on three principles:
1. **Shedding** — the external cladding sheds the majority of wind-driven rain
2. **Drainage** — any water that penetrates the outer skin is collected and drained to the outside via a drained cavity or flashing system
3. **Sealing** — the inner layer of the facade assembly provides a continuous air and vapour barrier to prevent residual moisture from reaching the interior

Effective facade weatherproofing requires coordination between multiple trades: the facade installer, the window installer, the services contractor (for penetrations), and the waterproofing contractor (for junctions with balconies and roofs). Failure at any one junction can lead to water ingress, and the source of leaks in a facade system can be extremely difficult to locate and repair once the building is occupied.

Reference: AS 4284 (Testing of building facades); NCC 2022, Volume 1 Part F1 (Damp and weatherproofing)
Applies to: Class 2, 3 buildings; all multi-residential project types
Related standards: AS 2047 (Windows and external glazed doors); AS 4420 (Windows test methods)

Common pitfalls:
- Not specifying and testing flashings at window-to-wall junctions, which is the most common location for facade water ingress in apartment buildings
- Relying solely on sealant (silicone or polyurethane) for weatherproofing at junctions, without a secondary drainage layer — sealant joints have a finite life and will eventually fail
- Not conducting facade testing (water spray testing to AS 4284 or hose testing) before handover, which means facade leaks are only discovered during the first heavy rain after occupation
- Failing to coordinate penetrations through the facade for services (dryer vents, AC pipes, exhaust fans), which are frequently installed without proper flashing or sealing

## Services Design for Apartments

### Vertical Risers and Service Distribution

The services distribution strategy in a multi-residential building is fundamentally different from commercial buildings because each apartment is a self-contained dwelling requiring its own metered services connections. The vertical distribution of services — water, sewer, gas, electricity, communications, and fire services — is managed through vertical risers that run through the building from the basement or ground-level plant rooms to each floor.

Riser design has significant implications for buildability, maintenance, and unit layout:
- **Location:** Risers should be located adjacent to the wet areas of each apartment (bathrooms, kitchens, laundries) to minimise horizontal pipe runs within the apartment. In many apartment layouts, the riser is located in a shared wall between two apartments, serving the wet areas of both units.
- **Size:** Riser shafts must be large enough to accommodate all services, with allowance for access panels, maintenance space, and fire-rated enclosures. Undersized risers are a common design coordination failure that leads to congested, unserviceable shafts.
- **Fire rating:** Risers penetrate fire-rated floors and must be enclosed by fire-rated construction with an FRL matching the floor. All penetrations through the riser walls and floors must be fire-stopped with tested and certified systems.
- **Access:** Risers must be accessible for maintenance and metering. Common approaches include access panels from common corridors or dedicated riser cupboards on each floor.

Hydraulic (water and sewer) risers require particular attention. Each apartment needs its own water sub-meter and isolation valve, accessible from the common corridor or riser cupboard. Sewer stacks must be vented to atmosphere, and the vent pipe must extend above the roof level. In taller buildings, water pressure management (pressure-reducing valves or zoned water supply) may be required to maintain appropriate pressure at all levels.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part G (Ancillary provisions — services and facilities); AS/NZS 3500.1 (Water services), AS/NZS 3500.2 (Sanitary plumbing and drainage)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: AS/NZS 3000 (Electrical installations — Wiring Rules); AS/NZS 3500 series (Plumbing and drainage)

Common pitfalls:
- Undersizing riser shafts during design, forcing services to be routed through apartment ceiling spaces or walls instead, increasing cost and reducing ceiling heights
- Not coordinating riser locations with apartment layouts, leading to long horizontal pipe runs, reduced ceiling heights, and noise transmission from pipes running through living areas
- Failing to provide adequate access to risers for maintenance, metering, and emergency shut-off — this is a common complaint from building managers
- Not fire-stopping riser penetrations through fire-rated floors, which is a recurring defect finding in apartment building inspections

### Metering and Utility Connections

Individual metering of utilities (electricity, water, gas, communications) is a fundamental requirement for apartment buildings where each SOU is a separate tenancy. The metering strategy must be coordinated with the utility providers, the building manager, and the strata management arrangements.

**Electricity metering:** Each apartment requires an individual electricity meter. In most jurisdictions, the electricity distributor requires meters to be located in a common meter room (or on each floor in a meter panel) accessible from the common area without entering any SOU. For larger buildings, the main switchboard and transformer are located at ground or basement level, with submains rising through the electrical risers to floor-level distribution boards. The sizing of the main switchboard and transformer depends on the total connected load of all apartments plus the common area services (lifts, pumps, lighting, car park ventilation, fire systems).

**Water metering:** Individual water metering for each apartment is required by most state water authorities for new Class 2 buildings. Sub-meters are typically installed in the hydraulic riser on each floor, with remote reading capability (either radio-frequency or pulse output to a building management system). A master meter at the boundary measures total building consumption, and the building manager reconciles the sum of sub-meter readings against the master meter to identify common area usage and unmetered losses.

**Gas metering:** Where gas is reticulated to individual apartments (for cooktops, hot water, or heating), individual gas meters are required. Gas meters are typically located externally (on the facade or in a ventilated meter enclosure at each level) due to the requirement for natural ventilation of the meter installation.

**Communications:** Each apartment requires separate connections for telephone, internet, and free-to-air television. The National Broadband Network (NBN) is the default communications provider for new buildings, and the developer must arrange for NBN infrastructure to be installed during construction. This includes fibre or hybrid fibre-coaxial cabling from the building entry point (network boundary) to each apartment.

Reference: AS/NZS 3000 (Electrical installations); AS/NZS 3500.1 (Water services); AS/NZS 5601.1 (Gas installations); AS/CA S009 (Cabling installation requirements)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: Utility provider connection requirements (varies by jurisdiction and provider)

Common pitfalls:
- Not engaging with the electricity distributor early enough to confirm transformer requirements and connection timelines — transformer procurement and installation can take 16-24 weeks and is on the critical path for energising the building
- Failing to coordinate the location and size of meter rooms with the architectural layout, leading to insufficient space for the number of meters required
- Not installing NBN infrastructure during construction, which requires retrofitting at significantly higher cost after the building is complete
- Underestimating the common area electrical load (lifts, car park ventilation, fire pumps, common lighting, pool/gym equipment), leading to an undersized main switchboard

### Mechanical Ventilation in Apartments

Natural ventilation of apartments is preferred under the NCC and state planning controls (particularly NSW's Apartment Design Guide), but many apartment layouts — especially those with single-aspect units, units facing noise sources, or units in tall buildings where openable windows are impractical — require mechanical ventilation.

NCC Volume 1, Part F4 (Light and ventilation) requires that every habitable room in an SOU has access to either natural ventilation (openable windows or doors with an aggregate opening area of not less than 5% of the floor area of the room) or mechanical ventilation complying with AS 1668.2 (Mechanical ventilation in buildings) or AS 1668.4 (Natural ventilation of buildings).

Bathrooms and laundries without openable windows require mechanical exhaust ventilation. The DTS solution is a continuously running exhaust fan or an intermittent fan controlled by the light switch, with minimum extract rates specified in AS 1668.2 (typically 25 L/s for a bathroom and 20 L/s for a toilet).

For apartments that rely on mechanical ventilation for habitable rooms, a centralised or semi-centralised system may be used. Common approaches include:
- **Individual split-system air conditioning** for each apartment, with outdoor units on the facade or rooftop — the most common solution for Australian apartments
- **Centralised condenser water loop systems** where each apartment has a water-cooled fan coil unit connected to a building-wide condenser water loop and cooling tower
- **Variable Refrigerant Flow (VRF) systems** with outdoor units serving multiple apartments

The choice of HVAC system has significant implications for capital cost, running cost, facade appearance, acoustic performance, and strata management complexity. Split systems are lowest capital cost but result in multiple outdoor units on the facade, which affects building appearance and can generate noise complaints. Centralised systems have higher capital cost but lower running costs and better aesthetic outcomes.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Part F4 (Light and ventilation); AS 1668.2 (Mechanical ventilation in buildings); AS 1668.4 (Natural ventilation of buildings)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: AS/NZS 3823 (Performance of air conditioners); state-specific apartment design guides (NSW ADG natural ventilation requirements)

Common pitfalls:
- Not meeting the 5% openable area requirement for natural ventilation, particularly in apartments with large floor-to-ceiling windows where the openable portion is a small fraction of the total glazing
- Locating split-system outdoor units without considering noise impact on adjacent apartments and neighbouring properties — outdoor unit noise frequently generates body corporate complaints
- Not providing dedicated exhaust air pathways from internal bathrooms and laundries, leading to moisture buildup, mould growth, and occupant health complaints
- Failing to account for the electrical load of split-system air conditioning units (typically 2-5 kW per unit) in the building's electrical design, which can overload the apartment circuit or the building's main switchboard

## Strata Title and Common Property

### Strata Title Overview

Almost all multi-residential apartment buildings in Australia are subdivided under strata title legislation, which creates individual lots (the apartments) and common property (shared areas such as lobbies, corridors, stairs, lifts, driveways, gardens, and pools). Understanding strata title is essential for project managers and quantity surveyors because it determines the scope of defect liability, the responsibility for maintenance, and the mechanisms for resolving building defects after completion.

Each state has its own strata legislation:
- NSW: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015 and Strata Schemes Development Act 2015
- VIC: Owners Corporations Act 2006
- QLD: Body Corporate and Community Management Act 1997
- WA: Strata Titles Act 1985
- SA: Strata Titles Act 1988
- TAS: Strata Titles Act 1998
- ACT: Unit Titles (Management) Act 2011
- NT: Unit Titles Act 1975

Despite the different legislative frameworks, the fundamental concepts are consistent across all jurisdictions: the building is subdivided into individual lots and common property, an owners corporation (body corporate) is established to manage the common property, and a levy system funds the ongoing maintenance and administration of the building.

For construction professionals, the strata plan defines the boundaries of each lot — and therefore the boundaries of the developer's defect liability for each purchaser. Work within the lot boundary (internal finishes, fixtures, appliances) is the developer's obligation to the individual lot owner. Work to common property (structure, common services, facade, common areas) is the developer's obligation to the owners corporation as a whole.

Reference: State strata legislation (NSW Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, VIC Owners Corporations Act 2006, QLD Body Corporate and Community Management Act 1997, etc.)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types involving strata subdivision
Related standards: Surveying and spatial data requirements for strata plan registration (varies by state)

Common pitfalls:
- Not understanding the strata plan boundaries when assessing defect liability — a defect in a common property element (e.g., a fire door in a corridor) is a claim by the owners corporation, not the individual lot owner
- Failing to register the strata plan before settlement of individual lots, which delays purchaser settlements and creates contractual complications
- Not establishing the initial maintenance fund (sinking fund) with adequate capital to cover the first years of maintenance, leading to special levies that create dissatisfaction among new purchasers

### Common Property Defects and Statutory Warranties

Building defect claims in strata-titled apartment buildings are governed by both the common law and statutory warranty schemes that vary by state. The most developed statutory scheme is in NSW, where the Home Building Act 1989 (as amended by the Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020) provides a comprehensive framework for defect claims.

In NSW, the statutory warranty periods are:
- **Major defects:** 6 years from completion (or the date of issue of the Occupation Certificate)
- **Other defects:** 2 years from completion
- **Structural defects:** covered under the major defects warranty (6 years)

A "major defect" is defined to include any defect in a major element of the building that is attributable to defective design, defective or faulty workmanship, defective materials, or a failure to comply with the structural requirements of the NCC. Major elements include internal and external load-bearing components, fire safety systems, waterproofing, and any element whose failure would be likely to cause the inability to inhabit or use the building for its intended purpose.

The Strata Building Bond and Inspections scheme (NSW, from 2020) requires developers of Class 2 buildings to lodge a building bond equal to 2% of the contract price. This bond can be claimed by the owners corporation to fund rectification of defects identified in interim and final defect inspections conducted within the first two years after completion.

Other states have different statutory warranty schemes with varying warranty periods and defect definitions. Victoria's Domestic Building Contracts Act 1995 provides a 10-year structural warranty and a 6-year warranty for other defects. Queensland's Building Act 1975 provides a 6-year structural warranty and a 1-year non-structural warranty.

Reference: NSW Home Building Act 1989; NSW Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020; VIC Domestic Building Contracts Act 1995; QLD Building Act 1975
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment projects subject to strata subdivision
Related standards: State building legislation and consumer protection frameworks

Common pitfalls:
- Not understanding the different warranty periods for major versus non-major defects, leading to claims being made outside the applicable limitation period
- Failing to maintain adequate defect rectification records, which are needed to demonstrate that defects were addressed and the warranty obligation was satisfied
- Not budgeting for the 2% strata building bond (NSW) in the project feasibility assessment, which is a significant cash flow impact for the developer
- Assuming that the defect liability period under the construction contract (typically 12 months DLP) is the same as the statutory warranty period, which in most states extends well beyond the contractual DLP

## Cost Planning for Multi-Residential Projects

### Feasibility Cost Benchmarks

Multi-residential projects require feasibility cost estimates that capture the full range of costs specific to apartment construction. The typical cost structure for a Class 2 apartment building in a major Australian metropolitan area includes:

**Construction cost benchmarks (indicative, 2024-2025 $/m2 of gross floor area):**
- Low-rise apartments (3-4 storeys, walk-up): $3,200 - $4,200/m2
- Mid-rise apartments (5-8 storeys): $3,800 - $5,000/m2
- High-rise apartments (9+ storeys): $4,500 - $6,500/m2
- Premium/luxury high-rise: $6,000 - $9,000+/m2

These rates include preliminaries and margin but exclude land, professional fees, authority charges, marketing, finance costs, and GST.

Key cost drivers that differentiate multi-residential from other project types:
- **Repetition factor:** Apartments benefit from repetitive construction — identical or similar floor plates allow for production-line efficiency in structure, services, and finishes. Non-standard floor plates (varying unit mixes, irregularly shaped buildings) reduce this benefit.
- **Number of wet areas per unit:** Bathrooms and kitchens are the most expensive rooms per square metre. A development with a high proportion of 2-bedroom/2-bathroom units will cost more per square metre than one with predominantly 1-bedroom/1-bathroom units.
- **Facade-to-floor ratio:** The cost of the facade is a significant proportion of total construction cost (typically 15-25%). Buildings with a high perimeter-to-area ratio (slender towers, irregular shapes) have higher facade costs per square metre of floor area.
- **Basement parking:** Basement car parking is typically $45,000-$65,000 per space (including excavation, retention, structure, waterproofing, ventilation, fire protection, and finishes). The number and depth of basement levels is a critical cost driver.
- **Common area quality:** Lobbies, corridors, lifts, pool, gym, and communal spaces represent a significant cost that does not generate directly saleable area. The ratio of sellable area to gross floor area (efficiency ratio) is typically 75-85% for well-designed apartment buildings.

Reference: Industry cost benchmarks (Rawlinsons, AIQS, Rider Levett Bucknall Oceania); project-specific feasibility analysis
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; new apartment developments
Related standards: AIQS Cost Management Practice Guide

Common pitfalls:
- Using $/m2 rates based on net sellable area rather than gross floor area (or vice versa) without clearly stating the measurement basis, leading to misleading feasibility results
- Not including basement car parking costs in the $/m2 benchmark, which can understate the construction cost by 10-20%
- Applying cost benchmarks from one market to another without adjusting for location factors (e.g., Sydney rates applied to a regional NSW project, or Melbourne rates applied to Perth)
- Not escalating cost benchmarks from the date of the benchmark data to the anticipated construction period, which can add 3-5% per annum in a rising market

### Trade Package Structure

Multi-residential projects are typically procured through trade package procurement, where the head contractor awards individual subcontracts for each trade. The typical trade package breakdown for an apartment building is:

**Early works packages (procured 16-20 weeks before site start):**
- Demolition and site clearing
- Bulk excavation and earthworks
- Shoring and retention (if deep basement)
- Piling (if required by geotechnical conditions)

**Structure packages (procured 12-16 weeks before trade commencement):**
- Concrete structure (formwork, reinforcement, concrete supply and placement)
- Structural steel (if applicable — typically for transfer structures, roof frames)
- Post-tensioning (if applicable — common for long-span floor plates and transfer slabs)

**Enclosure packages (procured 12-20 weeks before trade commencement):**
- Facade/cladding system
- Windows and glazing
- Roof waterproofing and roof plumbing
- Balcony waterproofing

**Services packages (procured 8-12 weeks before trade commencement):**
- Mechanical (HVAC)
- Electrical
- Hydraulic/plumbing
- Fire protection (sprinklers, hydrants, detection)
- Lifts (procured very early — 16-24 weeks lead time)
- Communications/data cabling

**Finishes packages (procured 4-8 weeks before trade commencement):**
- Internal partitions and ceiling lining (plasterboard)
- Tiling (bathrooms, kitchens, common areas)
- Joinery (kitchens, vanities, wardrobes)
- Stone benchtops
- Painting
- Floor coverings (carpet, timber, vinyl)
- Bathroom accessories
- Appliances
- Mirrors and shower screens

**External works packages:**
- Landscaping and paving
- Fencing and gates
- External lighting
- Pool and gym fit-out (if applicable)

Reference: Industry best practice; AIQS Cost Management Practice Guide
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: AS 4120 (Code of tendering)

Common pitfalls:
- Not procuring lifts early enough — lift manufacturing lead times of 16-24 weeks mean that the lift order should be placed before or at the time of concrete structure commencement
- Bundling facade and windows into a single package when they involve different supply chains, or splitting them when they should be coordinated under a single subcontractor
- Not identifying long-lead items (transformers, switchboards, facade panels, lifts, stone) early enough to order them before they become critical path items
- Underestimating the number and complexity of finishes packages in apartment projects — a typical apartment building may have 25-30 separate trade packages

## Quality and Defect Management

### Common Defect Categories in Apartment Buildings

Australian apartment buildings have faced significant scrutiny regarding construction quality following high-profile defect cases and the Building Confidence Report (Shergold-Weir, 2018). Understanding the most common defect categories allows project teams to focus quality management efforts on the areas of greatest risk.

The most common defect categories in Australian Class 2 buildings, ranked by frequency and cost impact, are:

1. **Waterproofing failures** — the most prevalent and costly defect category. Failures occur in wet areas (bathrooms, laundries), balconies, roofs, and planter boxes. Rectification requires stripping finishes, re-waterproofing, and reinstating — often across multiple levels when water cascades downward.

2. **Fire safety defects** — the second most consequential category due to life-safety implications. Common defects include unprotected service penetrations through fire-rated construction, missing or non-compliant fire doors, incomplete fire stopping at riser penetrations, and non-compliant smoke alarm installations.

3. **Facade and cladding defects** — water ingress at window-to-wall junctions, use of non-compliant cladding materials, inadequate weatherproofing at balcony-to-facade junctions, and cracking of rendered facades.

4. **Structural cracking** — cracking in concrete slabs, walls, and balconies due to inadequate reinforcement, insufficient curing, excessive deflection, or thermal movement. While structural cracking is not always a defect (some cracking is expected and managed through crack control reinforcement), excessive or uncontrolled cracking can compromise waterproofing and fire resistance.

5. **Acoustic performance failures** — inadequate sound insulation between apartments, particularly impact sound transmission through floors. These defects are difficult to rectify because they typically require removing and rebuilding floor or wall assemblies.

6. **Internal finishes defects** — tiling defects (cracked tiles, uneven surfaces, inadequate waterproofing under tiles), painting defects, joinery defects, and appliance failures. These are the most numerous but generally the least costly to rectify individually.

Reference: Building Confidence Report (Shergold-Weir, 2018); NSW Building Commissioner annual reports; Deakin University apartment building defects research
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: State building legislation; Home Building Act 1989 (NSW); Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020 (NSW)

Common pitfalls:
- Focusing quality inspections on visible finishes while neglecting concealed elements (waterproofing, fire stopping, acoustic insulation) that are the most costly defect categories
- Not conducting progressive inspections at critical hold points (before waterproofing is covered by tiles, before plasterboard covers fire stopping) when defects are still accessible and inexpensive to rectify
- Treating defect management as a post-completion activity rather than a proactive quality management process throughout construction
- Not maintaining a photographic record of concealed elements (waterproofing, fire stopping, insulation) before they are covered, which is essential for defending defect claims and demonstrating compliance

### Quality Hold Points for Apartment Construction

A quality hold point is a mandatory inspection stage at which work must stop until the relevant inspection has been completed and approved. Effective use of hold points is the single most important quality management tool for apartment construction, because the most costly defects involve concealed elements that cannot be inspected after subsequent trades have covered them.

The critical hold points for apartment construction are:

**Structural hold points:**
- Pre-pour inspection of reinforcement (position, size, spacing, cover, laps, tie wire) before each concrete pour
- Post-strip inspection of concrete surfaces (honeycombing, voids, cracks, dimensional tolerance)
- Structural steel connection inspection before encasement or fire protection

**Waterproofing hold points:**
- Substrate inspection before waterproofing membrane application (falls, surface preparation, priming)
- Membrane inspection after application and before tiling or other finishes (continuity, thickness, upturn heights, penetration treatment, flood testing)
- Balcony waterproofing inspection including falls, upturns, drainage, and threshold treatment

**Fire safety hold points:**
- Fire-rated wall and floor penetration inspection before concealment (fire collars, wraps, sealants at every penetration through fire-rated construction)
- Fire door installation inspection (frame fit, self-closer operation, intumescent seals, smoke seals, correct FRL tag)
- Fire stair inspection (FRL of enclosing construction, pressurisation provisions, signage, exit hardware)

**Services hold points:**
- Rough-in inspection for all services (position, support, insulation, access provision) before wall and ceiling lining
- Pressure testing of hydraulic systems before concealment
- Electrical rough-in inspection and insulation resistance testing

**Acoustic hold points:**
- Inter-tenancy wall and floor assembly inspection (correct stud arrangement, insulation type and thickness, number and type of plasterboard layers, junction treatment) before final lining

Reference: Industry best practice; state building legislation requirements for mandatory inspection stages
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types
Related standards: NCC 2022 mandatory notification stages; state-specific critical stage inspection requirements

Common pitfalls:
- Not enforcing hold points, allowing trades to proceed before inspections are completed — this is the primary cause of concealed defects
- Not documenting hold point inspections with photographs and sign-off records, which are needed to demonstrate compliance and defend warranty claims
- Having too few hold points (missing critical stages) or too many (creating program delays without commensurate quality benefit)
- Not empowering the quality team to stop work if a hold point inspection reveals non-compliant work — a culture of "fix it later" inevitably leads to defects that are never fixed

## Energy Efficiency and Sustainability

### NCC Energy Efficiency Requirements for Class 2

NCC Volume 1, Section J (Energy efficiency) establishes the energy efficiency requirements for Class 2 buildings. These requirements apply to the building envelope (external walls, roof, glazing, floor) and the building services (HVAC, lighting, hot water). The Section J provisions are a minimum standard — many projects exceed these requirements due to planning controls, Green Star ratings, or marketing differentiation.

The DTS energy efficiency provisions for Class 2 buildings include:
- **Building fabric:** Minimum R-values for external walls, roof, and floor assemblies, which vary by climate zone (Australia is divided into 8 climate zones for NCC purposes). Glazing must meet maximum U-value and solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC) limits based on the climate zone, orientation, and glazing area.
- **SOUs:** Each sole-occupancy unit must achieve a minimum NatHERS rating of 7 stars (on a scale of 0-10) for the combined heating and cooling energy. This requirement, introduced in NCC 2022, is a significant step up from the previous 6-star requirement and has implications for apartment layout, glazing selection, and insulation specification.
- **Common areas:** HVAC, lighting, and hot water systems serving common areas must meet the efficiency requirements of NCC Section J, which are aligned with ASHRAE 90.1 benchmarks.
- **Whole-of-building provisions:** NCC 2022 introduced a whole-of-building energy budget pathway that assesses the total energy consumption of the building including common area services, and allows trade-offs between building fabric and services efficiency.

State-specific requirements may exceed the NCC minimum:
- **NSW BASIX:** The Building Sustainability Index requires all new Class 2 developments to meet energy, water, and thermal comfort targets. BASIX energy targets are more stringent than the NCC minimum in many configurations.
- **VIC Better Apartments:** Energy efficiency provisions are part of the broader design quality standards.
- **Green Star ratings:** Many apartment developments voluntarily pursue Green Star ratings (typically 5-star or 6-star) for marketing differentiation or planning incentives.

Reference: NCC 2022, Volume 1, Section J (Energy efficiency); NatHERS rating methodology
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all new apartment developments
Related standards: BASIX (NSW); NatHERS (National House Energy Rating Scheme); Green Star Design & As Built rating tool

Common pitfalls:
- Not modelling the NatHERS rating for each unique apartment type early in design, leading to late-stage design changes to achieve the 7-star minimum
- Specifying large glazing areas without considering the impact on heating and cooling loads — floor-to-ceiling glazing is architecturally desirable but thermally challenging, particularly for west-facing apartments
- Not including the cost of energy efficiency measures (upgraded glazing, additional insulation, shading devices) in the feasibility cost estimate
- Treating NatHERS modelling as a compliance exercise performed at the end of design rather than a design tool used during concept and design development to optimise apartment performance

### Embedded Energy and Sustainability Considerations

Beyond code-mandated energy efficiency, multi-residential projects are increasingly expected to address broader sustainability objectives including embedded carbon, water efficiency, waste reduction, and electric vehicle charging infrastructure. These considerations affect both the capital cost and the long-term operating cost of the building.

Key sustainability considerations for apartment projects:
- **Electric vehicle charging:** Many planning authorities now require a proportion of car parking spaces to be EV-ready (with conduit and circuit capacity for future charger installation) or to have EV chargers installed. This adds electrical infrastructure cost and may increase the capacity of the building's main switchboard and transformer.
- **Solar PV and battery storage:** Rooftop solar panels can offset common area electricity consumption (lifts, car park lighting, pool and gym equipment). Battery storage can time-shift solar generation to evening peak demand. The economics depend on the available roof area (which competes with plant equipment, cooling towers, and communal rooftop amenities), the electricity tariff structure, and the strata management arrangements for cost allocation.
- **Water recycling:** Rainwater harvesting and greywater recycling systems can reduce potable water consumption for toilet flushing, irrigation, and car washing. These systems add capital cost and ongoing maintenance requirements but can significantly reduce water bills, particularly in drought-prone regions.
- **Waste management:** Multi-residential buildings require integrated waste management facilities including separate collection for general waste, recyclables, organic waste, and bulky waste. The design of waste storage rooms, chute systems (for taller buildings), and collection vehicle access is a planning and design coordination issue that is frequently under-resolved during design.

Reference: Industry sustainability frameworks; Green Star Design & As Built rating tool; state planning requirements for EV charging and sustainability
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; new apartment developments
Related standards: NCC 2022 Section J (Energy efficiency); BASIX (NSW); state-specific sustainability requirements

Common pitfalls:
- Not providing adequate electrical infrastructure for future EV charging, which is difficult and expensive to retrofit in a completed building
- Overestimating the financial return from rooftop solar on apartment buildings, where the available roof area per apartment is small compared to detached houses
- Not designing waste management facilities to meet council collection requirements, leading to non-functional waste systems and ongoing management issues
- Installing greywater or rainwater recycling systems without adequate maintenance plans, leading to system failures and health risks

## Project Delivery Considerations

### Apartment-Specific Programming Considerations

Multi-residential construction follows the "wedding cake" sequencing pattern, where multiple floors are under construction simultaneously at different stages of completion. This sequencing creates a production-line efficiency that is the key advantage of apartment construction — once the cycle time per floor is established, the program becomes predictable and repetitive.

The typical cycle time per floor for a medium-rise apartment building is 7-14 days, depending on the floor area and structural system. The critical path for most apartment buildings runs through the structural cycle — the speed at which the concrete frame can be constructed determines the overall project duration.

Key programming milestones and sequencing for apartment construction:
- **Structure** leads all other trades by 3-5 floors
- **Facade installation** follows structure by 3-5 floors (facade material procurement is typically a critical path item due to long lead times)
- **Building enclosure** (facade complete, waterproofing complete) is the milestone that triggers internal rough-in
- **Internal rough-in** (mechanical, electrical, hydraulic, fire) follows enclosure by 1-2 floors
- **Internal lining** (plasterboard) follows rough-in by 1 floor after services inspection
- **Wet area waterproofing** follows lining, with mandatory hold point for flood testing before tiling
- **Tiling** follows waterproofing approval
- **Second fix** (services fit-off, joinery, painting, floor coverings) follows lining completion
- **Commissioning** begins when the first floor of services is complete and progresses floor by floor
- **Defects inspection and rectification** begins from the top floor down (or the first completed floor) and runs concurrently with ongoing works on lower floors

**Vertical transport constraints:** During construction, the permanent lifts are not available (they are typically the last major system to be commissioned). All personnel, materials, and equipment must be transported vertically using temporary construction hoists. The number, capacity, and location of construction hoists is a critical programming and logistics consideration. A single hoist can become a bottleneck for the entire project if undersized or poorly located.

Reference: Industry best practice; multi-storey residential construction programming principles
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; new apartment projects of 3 or more storeys
Related standards: AS 4000-1997 (traditional procurement); AS 4902-2000 (D&C procurement)

Common pitfalls:
- Not establishing and monitoring the structural cycle time, which determines the overall program duration — a 2-day slip in the cycle time on a 20-storey building adds 40 days to the program
- Underestimating the time required for facade installation and building enclosure, which gates internal works — if the building is not enclosed, internal trades cannot start regardless of structural progress
- Not providing adequate construction hoists for the volume of personnel and materials required during peak fit-out, leading to hoists becoming a project bottleneck
- Programming commissioning as a single phase at the end of the project rather than a progressive floor-by-floor activity, which compresses an already constrained completion period

### Design and Construct (D&C) Delivery for Apartments

Design and construct (D&C) is the most common procurement method for multi-residential apartment projects in Australia. Under D&C, the developer engages a single head contractor who is responsible for both completing the design and constructing the building. This transfers design risk to the contractor and provides a lump sum price certainty that is attractive for developer-driven projects.

The typical D&C process for apartments:
1. **Concept design** — the developer engages architects and consultants to prepare a concept design sufficient to obtain planning approval (DA/Building Permit) and to define the product for marketing purposes
2. **D&C tender** — the concept design, design brief, and employer's requirements are issued to D&C contractors for tender. The contractor prices the project based on the concept design and proposes their design development approach
3. **Contract award** — the successful contractor is engaged under AS 4902-2000 (or a bespoke D&C contract) with a lump sum price
4. **Design development** — the contractor's design team develops the concept design to construction documentation standard, subject to the developer's approval at key design stages
5. **Construction** — the contractor constructs in accordance with the documentation they have developed

For the quantity surveyor, the key risk in D&C procurement is that the lump sum price is based on a concept design that will be developed by the contractor. The developer must clearly define the minimum standards, material specifications, and performance requirements in the employer's requirements document — anything not specified will be designed to the minimum NCC/DTS standard to minimise cost.

Reference: AS 4902-2000 (General conditions of contract for design and construct); Industry best practice for D&C procurement
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; developer-driven apartment projects
Related standards: AS 4120 (Code of tendering); AS 4122 (General conditions for engagement of consultants)

Common pitfalls:
- Issuing a D&C tender with insufficient design development, leading to tenders that are not comparable because each contractor has interpreted the design intent differently
- Not defining minimum standards and finishes in the employer's requirements, resulting in the contractor specifying the cheapest compliant option
- Failing to maintain design review and approval rights during the contractor's design development phase, allowing the design to diverge from the developer's intent
- Not retaining an independent superintendent or project manager to administer the D&C contract and protect the developer's interests during construction

### Handover and Defects Liability Period

The handover process for a strata-titled apartment building is more complex than for other project types because there are multiple stakeholders receiving the building: the developer (who is the contracting party), the individual lot purchasers (who take title to their apartments), and the owners corporation (which takes responsibility for common property).

The handover sequence typically involves:
1. **Practical completion** — the superintendent certifies that the works are practically complete (fit for occupation, notwithstanding minor omissions or defects). This triggers the commencement of the defects liability period under the construction contract (typically 12 months under AS 4000 or AS 4902).
2. **Occupation Certificate** — the building certifier/surveyor issues an Occupation Certificate confirming that the building complies with the approved plans and the NCC. This is a statutory requirement before the building can be lawfully occupied.
3. **Strata plan registration** — the strata plan is registered, creating the individual lots and common property.
4. **Individual apartment settlements** — purchasers settle and take possession of their apartments. Pre-settlement inspections by purchasers (or their building inspectors) are standard practice.
5. **Owners corporation establishment** — the owners corporation is established at the first annual general meeting, and common property management arrangements (including building management contracts) are put in place.
6. **Defect inspections** — the owners corporation commissions interim and final defect inspections (required by statute in NSW under the Strata Building Bond scheme) to identify defects in common property.
7. **Final completion** — at the end of the defects liability period, the contractor rectifies all identified defects, and the superintendent issues a final certificate. Retention monies held under the contract are released.

Reference: AS 4000-1997 Clause 35 (Practical completion), Clause 37 (Payment); state strata legislation; state building legislation (Occupation Certificate requirements)
Applies to: Class 2 buildings; all apartment project types involving strata subdivision
Related standards: NSW Strata Building Bond and Inspections scheme; state Home Building Acts

Common pitfalls:
- Not scheduling adequate time in the program for the Occupation Certificate approval process, which can take 4-8 weeks and requires all essential services certification, compliance certificates, and as-built documentation to be complete
- Failing to complete as-built documentation (drawings, operations and maintenance manuals, warranty certificates) before practical completion, which delays the Occupation Certificate and purchaser settlements
- Not budgeting for defect rectification during the defects liability period — a budget provision of 1-2% of the contract sum is typical for defect rectification in the 12 months after practical completion
- Underestimating the volume and complexity of individual apartment inspections by purchasers, which can generate hundreds of minor defect items that must be resolved before or shortly after settlement
