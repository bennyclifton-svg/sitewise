# Procurement & Tendering Guide

## 1. Procurement Methods and Strategies

### Construct-Only (Traditional) Procurement
Construct-Only procurement separates the design phase from the construction phase. The Principal (Client) engages consultants (architects, engineers) to complete the design fully before tendering the construction works. The Contractor prices a lump sum to build precisely what is documented, assuming no design responsibility.

This method provides the Principal with high certainty on final product quality and aesthetics but requires a longer overall project duration due to sequential phases. Cost certainty at tender is high, provided the design documentation is complete and coordinated. The Principal retains the risk for design errors, omissions, and coordination issues; if the architect's drawing conflicts with the structural engineer's drawing, the Contractor is entitled to a variation for the cost to resolve it.

Reference: Industry best practice (AIQS / RICS guidelines)
Applies to: All building classes, commonly used for high-end residential, bespoke architecture, and government infrastructure.
Related standards: AS 4000-1997, AS 2124-1992

Common pitfalls:
- Tendering on incomplete documents (e.g., 80% design), leading to extensive variations when the final 20% is released.
- Assuming the Contractor will resolve design coordination clashes without seeking additional cost or time.
- Commencing construction before authority approvals (like a Construction Certificate) are fully secured for the finalized design.

### Design and Construct (D&C) Procurement
Under Design and Construct (D&C), the Principal provides a brief or Principal's Project Requirements (PPR) and a preliminary/concept design. The Contractor takes single-point responsibility to both complete the detailed design and construct the project to meet the PPR.

This method transfers design risk (suitability for purpose, compliance, and coordination) to the Contractor. It allows for "fast-tracking," where construction on early trades (like earthworks) can begin while later trades (like finishes) are still being designed. Costs are fixed early, but the Principal yields control over design details and quality, as the Contractor will naturally design for constructability and cost-efficiency within the bounds of the PPR.

Reference: Industry best practice (AIQS / RICS guidelines)
Applies to: All building classes, highly common in commercial, industrial, apartments, and institutional.
Related standards: AS 4902-2000, AS 4300-1995

Common pitfalls:
- Drafting a weak or ambiguous PPR that fails to mandate specific quality standards, allowing the Contractor to substitute inferior materials.
- The Principal overly interfering in detailed design and issuing directions that constitute variations to the PPR, inadvertently taking design risk back.
- Failing to properly novate (transfer) the initial design consultants from the Principal to the Contractor, breaking the chain of design liability.

### Construction Management (CM) Procurement
In Construction Management, the Principal enters directly into multiple individual trade contracts (e.g., separate contracts with the concreter, electrician, and plumber). The Principal also engages a Construction Manager for a fee to coordinate these trades, manage the site, and administer the contracts as the Principal's agent.

This method offers maximum flexibility: design can happen concurrently with construction, and the Principal only pays the actual cost of the trade packages plus the CM fee (open book). However, the Principal carries the ultimate cost and time risk. There is no guaranteed lump sum maximum price upfront.

Reference: Industry best practice (AIQS / RICS guidelines)
Applies to: Complex refurbishments, urgent works, or projects where scope cannot be defined upfront.
Related standards: AIA Construction Management Contract, bespoke forms.

Common pitfalls:
- The Principal lacking the internal capability to execute and manage dozens of direct trade contracts.
- Using CM when budget certainty is required by lenders, as there is no fixed price constraint.
- Blurred lines of liability when a trade contractor causes a defect; the CM manages the rectification but is usually not financially liable for trade defaults.

### Managing Contractor Procurement
Managing Contractor procurement is a hybrid where a head contractor is appointed early (often pre-design completion) to manage the design development and then construction. The Managing Contractor subcontracts out the physical work, but unlike CM, the subcontracts are held by the Managing Contractor, not the Principal.

It is typically structured in two phases: Phase 1 (Design/Planning) for a fixed fee, and Phase 2 (Construction) where a Target Outturn Cost (TOC) or Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP) is agreed. If costs exceed the GMP, the Managing Contractor bears the overrun; if savings are achieved, they are often shared. All subcontract procurement is strictly open-book and transparent to the Principal.

Reference: Government procurement guidelines (e.g., Defence standard forms)
Applies to: Large-scale, complex infrastructure, hospitals, and high-risk government projects.
Related standards: Bespoke government forms (e.g., MCC-1 2003), specialized AS suites.

Common pitfalls:
- Failing to clearly define what constitutes an allowable cost reimbursable to the Managing Contractor versus what is covered in their management fee.
- Negotiating the Target Outturn Cost too late in the process, leaving the Principal with little leverage if the price is unacceptable.
- Inadequate auditing by the Principal of the open-book trade procurement process.

### Early Contractor Involvement (ECI)
ECI is not a standalone procurement method but a phase enacted prior to a traditional or D&C contract. A contractor is engaged during the design phase under a consultancy agreement to provide buildability advice, value engineering, programming, and cost planning.

The goal is to derisk the project before entering a lump sum contract. At the end of the ECI phase, the contractor typically submits a binding offer to construct the works. If acceptable, the parties execute a D&C or Construct-Only contract. If unacceptable, the Principal owns the intellectual property and can tender the design to the open market.

Reference: Industry best practice (AIQS / RICS guidelines)
Applies to: Complex projects requiring logistical planning or specialized construction methodology (e.g., difficult site access, heritage retention).
Related standards: Bespoke ECI agreements.

Common pitfalls:
- Not clearly defining the IP ownership of ECI deliverables if the parties fail to agree on a construction contract price.
- The contractor "lowballing" the ECI fee to win the role, then inflating the construction offer knowing the Principal faces delays if they go to open tender.
- Failing to establish an independent cost benchmarking process (e.g., an independent QS) to verify the contractor's final offer is at market rates.

### Alliance Contracting
Alliance Contracting integrates the Principal, Contractor, and key consultants into a single team sharing project risks and rewards equally. It operates on a "no blame, no dispute" ethos. All direct, indirect, and overhead costs are reimbursed on an open-book basis.

A Target Cost is established. If the outturn cost is lower, the savings are shared according to a pre-agreed formula. If the cost overruns, the pain is shared (though usually capped for the non-owner participants at their profit margin). It is used exclusively for extraordinarily complex projects where risks are unquantifiable or shifting them to one party would result in prohibitive risk premiums.

Reference: National Alliance Contracting Guidelines (Infrastructure Australia)
Applies to: Mega-infrastructure, rail, water treatment, projects >$100M with massive unknown risks.
Related standards: Project Alliance Agreements (PAA).

Common pitfalls:
- Using an Alliance for a straightforward project, resulting in massive administrative overhead for no benefit.
- Selecting partners based solely on price capability rather than actual cultural alignment and collaborative behaviors.
- Setting the Target Cost too easily achievable, resulting in windfall gains for the participants without driving true innovation or efficiency.

## 2. Standard Forms of Contract in Australia

### Comparing AS 4000-1997 and AS 2124-1992
AS 4000-1997 (General conditions of contract) and AS 2124-1992 are the two foundational construct-only standard forms in Australia. Despite AS 4000 being created to supersede AS 2124, both remain heavily used.

**AS 2124-1992** is generally considered more Principal-friendly. It allows the Superintendent broad discretion, specifically allowing the Superintendent to grant an Extension of Time (EOT) even if the Contractor failed to claim it (Clause 35.5). The notification requirements for Contractor claims are strict but less absolute than AS 4000. It is heavily entrenched in government and institutional procurement.

**AS 4000-1997** is considered more balanced. It introduced the concept of time bars—if the Contractor fails to submit a claim within 28 days, the entitlement is expressly lost (Clause 41.2). It mandates that the Superintendent act fairly and reasonably. It also streamlined the variations process and introduced provisions for concurrent delay (apportionment). 

Reference: Standards Australia AS 4000-1997, AS 2124-1992
Applies to: Construct-Only procurement
Related standards: Subcontract forms AS 4901-1998 (for AS 4000) and AS 2545-1993 (for AS 2124)

Common pitfalls:
- Mixing terminology between the two (e.g., using "Practical Completion" definitions from AS 2124 in an AS 4000 contract).
- Attempting to administer an AS 2124 contract strictly under the Security of Payment legislation without understanding where the contract's lengthy certification periods clash with statutory maximums.

### AS 4902-2000 (Design and Construct) Structure
AS 4902-2000 is the standard form for Design and Construct procurement, built upon the framework of AS 4000. It introduces specific mechanisms to handle the design phase.

Key features include the **Principal's Project Requirements (PPR)**, which defines the brief, and the **Preliminary Design**, which forms part of the tender. The Contractor takes on absolute liability to ensure their finalized design satisfies the PPR and is fit for purpose (Clause 2.2). It includes a process for the Contractor to submit design documents to the Superintendent for review prior to construction. Crucially, the Superintendent's review does not relieve the Contractor of their design liability.

Reference: Standards Australia AS 4902-2000
Applies to: Design and Construct procurement
Related standards: Subcontract form AS 4903-2000

Common pitfalls:
- Creating conflicts by including highly detailed, prescriptive drawings in the PPR, confusing whether it is a performance specification (D&C) or a prescriptive specification (Construct-Only).
- The Superintendent approving Contractor designs rather than merely noting "no objection", potentially inadvertently taking on design liability.
- Failing to formally novate the Principal's early design consultants, leaving a gap where the Contractor does not hold a direct contract with the original engineers.

### ABIC Contracts (Architect Administered)
The Australian Building Industry Contracts (ABIC) suite is jointly published by Master Builders Australia (MBA) and the Australian Institute of Architects (AIA). They are designed specifically for architect-administered projects.

The **ABIC MW** (Major Works) and **ABIC SW** (Simple Works) forms are heavily used in private commercial and high-end residential sectors. Distinctively, the architect acts as the administrator (replacing the Superintendent). The ABIC forms have unique mechanisms for dealing with prime cost and provisional sums, and they heavily integrate the architect's instructions into the variations process. They are generally considered fair to both parties but architect-centric in process.

Reference: ABIC Suite (MW, SW, BW)
Applies to: Architect-designed and administered commercial and residential projects.
Related standards: None directly intersecting with AS standards.

Common pitfalls:
- Using an ABIC form when a non-architect (e.g., an independent Project Manager) is engaged to administer the contract. The contract relies on architectural certification.
- Misunderstanding the strict ABIC process for adjusting Provisional Sums, which differs substantially from the AS 4000 approach.
- Failing to use the mandated ABIC pro-forma notices, risking invalidation of claims.

## 3. The Tendering Process Flow

### Expression of Interest (EOI) Phase
For large or complex projects, procurement begins with an Expression of Interest (EOI), sometimes called a Request for Information (RFI - distinct from technical RFIs during tender). This is a public or targeted advertisement seeking responses from the market regarding capability, capacity, and interest.

The EOI does not ask for pricing. It evaluates qualitative measures: financial stability, relevant past experience, WHS records, and key personnel availability. The goal is to shortlist 3 to 5 highly capable contractors who will then be invited to participate in the Request for Tender (RFT) phase, ensuring the RFT evaluation is manageable and competitive tension is high.

Reference: Government Procurement Guidelines, AIQS Tendering Practice
Applies to: High-value, complex, or public sector projects.
Related standards: AS 4120-1994 (Code of tendering)

Common pitfalls:
- Writing an EOI criteria so narrow that only one or two firms qualify, instantly killing competitive tension for the RFT.
- Shortlisting too many contractors (e.g., 8+), which discourages top-tier firms from bidding due to the low statistical probability of winning versus the high cost of tendering.
- Asking for preliminary pricing in an EOI, muddying the waters between an open market sounding and a formal tender.

### Open vs. Select Tendering
**Open Tendering** involves advertising the project publicly and allowing any contractor to submit a bid. It is mandatory for most government projects above a certain threshold to ensure probity and equal opportunity. It ensures the lowest possible market price but requires immense effort to evaluate vastly different capabilities, and risks awarding to an incompetent low-bidder.

**Select Tendering** involves solely inviting a pre-qualified shortlist of contractors (usually 3 to 5) to bid. This is standard in the private sector. It guarantees that whichever firm wins has already been vetted for capability, so the evaluation can focus primarily on price and methodology. It reduces evaluation time and gives bidders confidence they have a realistic chance.

Reference: AS 4120-1994 (Code of tendering)
Applies to: All projects. Open for public sector; Select for private sector.
Related standards: Government procurement frameworks.

Common pitfalls:
- In select tendering, inviting contractors of vastly different sizes/tiers (e.g., a tier 1 multi-national against a local mom-and-pop builder). Their overhead structures are incomparable.
- In open tendering, failing to clearly define mandatory minimum criteria (e.g., specific licenses or ISO accreditations), leading to dozens of unusable submissions.
- "Making up the numbers" in select tendering by inviting a firm you have no intention of using just to show three prices to a financier.

### Tender Period Administration and Probity
The tender period is the time between issuing the RFT and the closing date. Managing this period requires strict discipline to ensure fairness (probity).
- **Requests for Information (RFIs)**: Tenderers submit questions about document discrepancies. Responses must be anonymized and circulated to ALL tenderers so everyone prices the same information.
- **Addenda**: Formal changes to the tender documents are issued as Addenda. They must be numbered and formally acknowledged by bidders in their returnable schedules.
- **Site Briefing / Walkthrough**: Usually mandatory for complex or refurbishment works. Notes from the briefing should be circulated.
- **Probity**: Absolute confidentiality must be maintained. No single bidder can receive preferential information. No pricing or identities can be disclosed prior to award.

Reference: AS 4120-1994 (Code of tendering)
Applies to: All projects, strictly enforced in government.
Related standards: N/A

Common pitfalls:
- Answering a bidder's question over the phone without issuing the clarification to the rest of the tenderers.
- Issuing massive design changes via an Addendum two days prior to the tender closing without extending the closing date, resulting in rushed, heavily padded pricing.
- Failing to establish an independent Probity Advisor on major public infrastructure projects to oversee the communications protocol.

## 4. Tender Documentation and Deliverables

### Structuring a Request for Tender (RFT) Package
A comprehensive RFT package ensures bidders have all information required to accurately assess risk and price the works. A standard package consists of:
1. **Conditions of Tender**: The rules of the game (closing time, lodgement format, evaluation criteria, validity period).
2. **Draft Conditions of Contract**: The formal agreement (e.g., AS 4000) heavily marked up with Special Conditions shifting risk.
3. **Specifications**: Detailed technical requirements broken down by trade, dictating materials, standards, and execution methods.
4. **Drawings / Design Docs**: All architectural, structural, civil, and services plans.
5. **Information Documents**: Background reports (geotechnical, hazardous materials, survey) provided for information but often excluded from the contract documents to push latent condition risk to the contractor.
6. **Returnable Schedules**: Standard forms the bidder must complete.

Reference: AIQS / RICS Standard Documentation structures
Applies to: All construct-only and D&C projects.
Related standards: AS 4120-1994

Common pitfalls:
- Leaving the Special Conditions of Contract as "to be negotiated," which forces tenderers to price a worst-case scenario risk profile.
- Including out-of-date or "Not For Construction" stamped drawings in an RFT for a Construct-Only project, making a lump sum legally unenforceable.
- Burying critical scope items purely in the Specification without showing them on the Drawings, leading to contractor omissions.

### Essential Returnable Schedules
Returnable schedules force bidders to present their pricing and methodology in identical formats, enabling "apples-to-apples" comparison. Essential schedules include:
- **Form of Tender**: A legally binding declaration of the lump sum price, contract validity period (usually 60-90 days), and agreement to the terms.
- **Schedule of Prices / Trade Breakdown**: The lump sum broken down into trades (concrete, electrical, preliminaries, margin). Vital for assessing progress claims later.
- **Schedule of Rates for Variations**: Pre-agreed hourly rates for trades, labor, and percentage markups for overhead and profit to govern future variations.
- **Proposed Key Personnel**: Resumes for the Project Manager, Site Manager, and Contract Administrator.
- **Proposed Construction Program**: A Gantt chart showing critical path, milestones, and site establishment.
- **Schedule of Departures/Qualifications**: The only place the bidder is legally allowed to nominate deviations from the RFT requirements.

Reference: AS 4120-1994, standard template libraries.
Applies to: All projects.
Related standards: N/A

Common pitfalls:
- Not requiring a Trade Breakdown, meaning the Principal has no baseline to evaluate the first progress claim and risks over-certifying early payments.
- Accepting a Schedule of Rates with exorbitant overhead / margin percentages (e.g., 25%), which will make every variation financially ruinous.
- Failing to mandate that the Proposed Key Personnel cannot be swapped out without Principal approval, resulting in "bait and switch" baiting with A-team resumes but deploying the C-team.

### Risk Allocation and Baseline Reports
A central function of the RFT is allocating risk. The general principle is that risk should be allocated to the party best equipped to manage it.
- **Geotechnical Risk (Ground Conditions)**: Often heavily contested. Principals provide a borehole report but formulate the contract to say the contractor assumes all risk for conditions not explicitly in the report.
- **Weather Delay Risk**: Contracts delineate standard inclement weather (contractor risk) vs exceptional weather events (shared risk or EOT entitlement).
- **Hazardous Materials / Authority Risk**: Contamination and planning approvals are typically retained by the Principal, as they are largely uncontrollable by the contractor.

The RFT must clearly define the *Baseline*. If the contractor is taking design risk via D&C, they are responsible for compliance, but only to the baseline standard established in the Principal's Project Requirements.

Reference: Contracting best practice (Infrastructure Australia guidelines).
Applies to: Contract drafting phase.
Related standards: Base AS forms (Latent conditions clauses).

Common pitfalls:
- Unilaterally shifting all risk for underground latent conditions onto the contractor on a CBD site without a geotechnical baseline, forcing them to price a massive blind contingency.
- Including a "fitness for purpose" warranty in the contract while simultaneously enforcing highly prescriptive, non-negotiable design details that contradict that purpose.

## 5. Tender Evaluation and Award

### The Tender Evaluation Matrix
Evaluating a tender strictly on the bottom-line price exposes the Principal to immense risk of contractor default, program delays, or massive variation claims. A Tender Evaluation Matrix weights price against qualitative factors.

A typical weighting for a private commercial project:
- **Price (50-60%)**: Lump sum, variation rates, prime cost allowances.
- **Program & Methodology (15-20%)**: Realistic critical path, site staging, logistics.
- **Capability & Relevant Experience (15-20%)**: Past similar projects, financial stability, WHS.
- **Contract Departures (10%)**: The financial and legal risk of the contractor's proposed amendments to the contract.

Tenders are scored blindly on qualitative criteria first, then combined with the quantitative price score to determine the "Value for Money" ranking.

Reference: Government Procurement Guidelines, Probity Frameworks
Applies to: All projects, mandatory documented process for government.
Related standards: AS 4120-1994

Common pitfalls:
- Reverse-engineering the evaluation matrix scores to justify awarding the project to a pre-determined favorite contractor (a major probity breach).
- Treating all schedules of rates equally without analyzing them against the likely frequency of those variations (e.g., high rock excavation rates matter heavily on a steep site).
- Ignoring the financial capability check and awarding to the lowest bidder who subsequently goes into administration mid-project.

### Normalizing Qualifications and Departures
Hardly any contractor submits a fully conforming bid on a major project. They submit "Qualifications" or "Departures" (e.g., "We exclude the cost of rock excavation", "We require the delay damages to be capped", "We are substituting Brand X aircon for Brand Y").

To evaluate fairly, the Principal's Quantity Surveyor must **Normalize** the bids. 
- If Bidder A includes rock excavation ($100k) and Bidder B excludes it, the QS adds $100k to Bidder B's price for evaluation purposes.
- If a legal departure shifts risk back to the Principal, a financial premium risk allocated.
This ensures the final comparison is strictly "apples to apples".

Reference: AIQS Cost Management Manual
Applies to: Post-tender evaluation analysis.
Related standards: Contract administration principles.

Common pitfalls:
- Comparing Bidder A's unqualified price against Bidder B's vastly lower, heavily qualified price without normalizing, creating a false impression of savings.
- Discarding an incredibly innovative alternative methodology simply because it is technically "non-conforming".
- Failing to explicitly reject unacceptable qualifications in the final contract execution, inadvertently incorporating them into the agreement.

### Letter of Award vs. Letter of Intent
Once a preferred tenderer is selected through evaluation and negotiation, they must be formally engaged.

**Letter of Award (LOA) / Letter of Acceptance**: A legally binding document that unconditionally accepts the contractor's offer (as amended by negotiations). It creates a binding contract immediately, even before the formal AS4000/AS4902 document is compiled and signed.

**Letter of Intent (LOI)**: A non-binding expression of intention to enter into a contract in the future. It is used to give the contractor comfort to start ordering long-lead materials or mobilizing, often with a capped expenditure limit, while the final contract details are hashed out. It is fraught with legal risk.

Reference: Construction Law principles.
Applies to: Contract execution phase.
Related standards: Contract formation elements (offer and acceptance).

Common pitfalls:
- Drafting a "Letter of Intent" that inadvertently contains all the legal elements of an absolute acceptance, binding the Principal to a contract they didn't intend to finalize yet.
- Allowing the contractor to commence full construction on site under a limited Letter of Intent, exhausting the funding cap and leaving the works without a governing contract framework.
- Failing to annex the negotiated schedule of agreed departures to the Letter of Award, allowing the contractor's original overarching qualifications to govern.
