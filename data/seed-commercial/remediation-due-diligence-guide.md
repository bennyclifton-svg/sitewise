# Remediation & Due Diligence Guide

## Due Diligence Process Overview

Environmental due diligence is the structured process of investigating the contamination status and environmental risks of a site before acquisition, development, or change of use. In Australian construction and property development, due diligence is not merely a commercial best practice — it is often a regulatory requirement triggered by planning controls, environmental legislation, and lending conditions.

The purpose of environmental due diligence is threefold: to identify whether the site is contaminated, to assess whether contamination poses a risk to human health or the environment under the proposed use, and to estimate the cost and timeframe for any remediation required to make the site suitable for its intended purpose. For a project manager or quantity surveyor, the financial implications of contamination can be transformative — remediation costs can range from negligible (minor surface clean-up) to catastrophic (multi-million dollar groundwater treatment programs that extend over decades).

The standard due diligence pathway in Australian practice follows a phased investigation approach:

1. **Phase 1 — Preliminary Site Assessment (PSA)** — desktop study and site walkover to identify potential contamination sources, pathways, and receptors. No intrusive sampling.
2. **Phase 2 — Detailed Site Investigation (DSI)** — intrusive investigation with soil, groundwater, and/or soil vapour sampling to confirm whether contamination exists and at what concentrations.
3. **Phase 3 — Remediation Action Plan (RAP)** — if contamination is confirmed above applicable criteria, a plan is developed to manage or remediate the contamination to make the site suitable for the proposed use.
4. **Phase 4 — Remediation and Validation** — execution of the RAP, followed by validation sampling to confirm the site meets the remediation criteria.
5. **Phase 5 — Site Audit (where required)** — independent review by an accredited site auditor who certifies that the site is suitable for the proposed use, with or without conditions.

This phased approach is designed to be proportionate — each phase is only triggered if the preceding phase identifies a need for further investigation. A site with no identified contamination history may only require a Phase 1 assessment. A former industrial site with known chemical storage will typically require all five phases.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 1999 (amended 2013), Schedule A — Framework for the assessment of site contamination
Applies to: All project types involving site acquisition, change of use, or development on potentially contaminated land
Related standards: State-specific contaminated land legislation (see State Regulatory Frameworks section)

Common pitfalls:
- Skipping the Phase 1 assessment and proceeding directly to intrusive investigation, which wastes money if the site has no contamination history and fails to identify all potential contaminant sources
- Treating due diligence as a box-ticking exercise rather than a genuine risk assessment, leading to inadequate investigation scope
- Not engaging a suitably qualified environmental consultant (typically a contaminated land specialist with relevant state accreditation or EIANZ certification)
- Completing due diligence after the property transaction is unconditional, leaving no contractual mechanism to address contamination found later

## Preliminary Site Assessment (Phase 1)

A Preliminary Site Assessment (PSA), also known as a Phase 1 Environmental Site Assessment, is a desktop and visual investigation to identify the potential for contamination at a site. It does not involve intrusive sampling — its purpose is to determine whether further investigation is warranted and, if so, to inform the scope of any subsequent detailed investigation.

The standard scope of a Phase 1 PSA includes:

**Desktop review:**
- Historical aerial photographs to identify past land uses (industrial activity, fuel storage, waste disposal, agriculture)
- Council records including development applications, building approvals, and any contamination notices
- EPA contaminated sites register and notifications (each state maintains its own register)
- Title searches and historical ownership records
- SafeWork/WorkCover records for hazardous materials (particularly asbestos)
- Geological and hydrogeological mapping to understand soil types, groundwater depth, and flow direction
- Previous environmental reports for the site or adjacent properties

**Site inspection:**
- Visual identification of potential contamination indicators (stained soil, distressed vegetation, chemical odours, underground storage tank fill points, waste materials)
- Identification of above-ground and below-ground storage tanks
- Assessment of building materials for potential asbestos-containing materials (ACM)
- Observation of adjacent land uses that could be contamination sources (e.g., service stations, dry cleaners, industrial facilities)
- Noting of surface water features, drainage patterns, and proximity to sensitive receptors

**Preliminary conceptual site model (CSM):**
The Phase 1 assessment should develop a preliminary conceptual site model identifying the source-pathway-receptor linkages. A CSM describes: what contaminants may be present (sources), how they could migrate (pathways — through soil, groundwater, vapour), and what they could affect (receptors — human health, ecological systems, groundwater resources). If no complete source-pathway-receptor linkage exists, the contamination risk may be low even if contaminants are present on site.

The Phase 1 report concludes with a recommendation on whether a Phase 2 detailed investigation is required. If no contamination indicators are found and the site history does not suggest any potentially contaminating activities, the Phase 1 may be sufficient to demonstrate the site is suitable for the proposed use. However, this determination depends on the proposed land use sensitivity and the applicable planning controls.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule A, Section 2 — Preliminary site investigation
Applies to: All sites being assessed for contamination potential, particularly prior to property transactions or development applications
Related standards: AS 4482.1-2005 (Guide to the investigation and sampling of sites with potentially contaminated soil — Non-volatile and semi-volatile compounds); AS 4482.2-1999 (Volatile substances)

Common pitfalls:
- Relying solely on the current site condition without researching historical uses — contamination from past activities can persist in soil and groundwater for decades
- Not reviewing aerial photographs back to the earliest available imagery (typically 1930s-1950s in Australian capital cities)
- Failing to investigate off-site sources of contamination, particularly upgradient properties that could impact the subject site through groundwater migration
- Not including a site inspection as part of the Phase 1, relying solely on desktop review
- Omitting the conceptual site model, which is essential for designing an appropriate Phase 2 investigation if one is needed

## Detailed Site Investigation (Phase 2)

A Detailed Site Investigation (DSI), also known as a Phase 2 Environmental Site Assessment, involves intrusive sampling and laboratory analysis to determine whether contamination is present, its nature, extent, and concentration. The DSI is designed based on the findings of the Phase 1 PSA and the preliminary conceptual site model.

The key components of a Phase 2 DSI are:

**Sampling and Analysis Quality Plan (SAQP):**
Before any fieldwork, a SAQP must be prepared that defines the investigation objectives, sampling locations and rationale, sampling methods, analytical suite, quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) requirements, and data quality objectives. The SAQP ensures the investigation is systematic, defensible, and produces data of sufficient quality for decision-making.

**Soil investigation:**
- Test pit excavation (typically 0.5-3m depth) or borehole drilling (for deeper profiles or where groundwater investigation is required)
- Systematic grid-based sampling and/or targeted sampling at areas of identified concern
- Field screening using photoionisation detectors (PIDs) for volatile organic compounds
- Laboratory analysis for the contaminants of potential concern (COPCs) identified in the Phase 1

**Groundwater investigation:**
- Installation of groundwater monitoring wells if the Phase 1 identified potential for groundwater contamination
- Measurement of groundwater depth, flow direction, and hydraulic gradient
- Groundwater sampling and laboratory analysis
- Assessment of whether contaminated groundwater could migrate off-site or impact sensitive receptors

**Soil vapour investigation:**
- Installation of soil vapour probes if volatile contaminants are suspected (petroleum hydrocarbons, chlorinated solvents, PFAS)
- Soil vapour sampling and analysis for comparison against Health Screening Levels (HSLs) in the NEPM

**Data assessment:**
- Comparison of analytical results against NEPM investigation levels (HILs, HSLs, EILs, GILs) for the proposed land use
- Refinement of the conceptual site model based on actual data
- Assessment of whether contamination concentrations exceed the applicable criteria for the proposed land use
- Determination of whether further investigation, risk assessment, or remediation is required

The Phase 2 report must clearly state whether the site is suitable for the proposed use without further action, or whether additional investigation, risk assessment, or remediation is needed.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule A, Section 3 — Detailed site investigation; Schedule B — Guidelines on investigation levels, laboratory analysis, and ecological investigation
Applies to: Sites where Phase 1 has identified potential contamination requiring confirmation
Related standards: AS 4482.1-2005, AS 4482.2-1999, AS/NZS 5667.1 (Water quality sampling guidance)

Common pitfalls:
- Insufficient sampling density — too few samples may fail to delineate the extent of contamination, leading to underestimation of remediation costs
- Not analysing for the full suite of contaminants of potential concern identified in the Phase 1 conceptual site model
- Failing to install groundwater monitoring wells when the Phase 1 identified potential for groundwater contamination
- Not collecting QA/QC samples (field duplicates, trip blanks, rinsate blanks) as required by the NEPM, which can undermine the defensibility of the data
- Using investigation levels for the current land use rather than the proposed land use — if the site is being rezoned from industrial to residential, the more conservative residential investigation levels apply

## Contamination Types in Australian Construction

Understanding the common types of contamination encountered in Australian construction is essential for project managers, quantity surveyors, and environmental consultants. The type of contamination determines the investigation approach, the applicable regulatory criteria, the remediation technology, and ultimately the cost.

**Petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH, BTEX, PAHs):**
The most commonly encountered contaminants in Australian urban areas. Sources include underground storage tanks (USTs) at service stations, fuel depots, transport depots, and commercial/industrial premises. The key analytes are total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH), benzene-toluene-ethylbenzene-xylene (BTEX), and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). BTEX compounds are of particular concern because they are volatile, mobile in groundwater, and carcinogenic (particularly benzene). Petroleum hydrocarbon contamination typically presents as light non-aqueous phase liquid (LNAPL) floating on the groundwater table, dissolved phase contamination in groundwater, and soil vapour intrusion risk.

**Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, chromium, mercury, cadmium, zinc, copper, nickel):**
Common on former industrial sites, railway corridors, smelter areas, and sites with a history of waste fill. In residential areas, lead contamination is frequently associated with historical use of lead-based paints and proximity to smelters or heavily trafficked roads (from leaded petrol, phased out in Australia in 2002). Arsenic contamination is found in areas with historical use of arsenic-based pesticides (particularly orchards and dipping sites), CCA-treated timber manufacturing, and mining. Heavy metals are generally immobile in soil (they do not migrate readily) but can be mobilised under acidic conditions or when soil is disturbed.

**Asbestos:**
Asbestos-containing materials (ACM) are ubiquitous in Australian buildings constructed before the mid-1980s and can be present in buildings up to 2003 (when the total ban on asbestos importation and use took effect). Asbestos in soil is commonly found on demolition sites, former building sites, and in fill material. The NEPM provides specific guidance for assessment of asbestos in soil, distinguishing between bonded (non-friable) asbestos and friable asbestos (which is more hazardous due to fibre release potential). Asbestos contamination is assessed based on the weight percentage and fibre concentration in soil, with investigation levels dependent on the proposed land use and potential for disturbance.

**Chlorinated solvents (TCE, PCE, DCE, vinyl chloride):**
Found on former dry cleaning premises, metal degreasing operations, electronics manufacturing, and some industrial sites. Chlorinated solvents are dense non-aqueous phase liquids (DNAPLs) that sink through the groundwater table and can contaminate large volumes of soil and groundwater. They are among the most difficult and expensive contaminants to remediate because of their persistence, mobility, and the difficulty of recovering DNAPL from the subsurface. Vinyl chloride, a degradation product of TCE and PCE, is a known human carcinogen.

**Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS):**
An emerging class of contaminants that has become one of the most significant environmental contamination issues in Australia. Sources include airports and defence bases (where PFAS-containing aqueous film-forming foam — AFFF — was used for firefighting training), industrial sites using PFAS in manufacturing, and wastewater treatment plants. PFAS are extremely persistent in the environment ("forever chemicals"), highly mobile in groundwater, and bio-accumulative. The Australian PFAS National Environmental Management Plan (PFAS NEMP) provides the national guidance framework. PFAS contamination can affect property values significantly, even at low concentrations, due to community concern and regulatory uncertainty.

**Pesticides and herbicides (organochlorine pesticides — OCPs, organophosphate pesticides — OPPs):**
Found on former agricultural land, orchards, market gardens, sheep and cattle dipping sites, and timber treatment facilities. Organochlorine pesticides (DDT, dieldrin, chlordane, heptachlor) are persistent in soil and can remain at elevated concentrations for decades after application ceased. These compounds are commonly encountered when agricultural land is rezoned for residential development.

**Fill material and waste:**
Many urban sites in Australia have been subject to uncontrolled filling, particularly before environmental regulations were established. Fill material can contain a wide range of contaminants including building demolition waste, industrial waste, domestic waste, and contaminated soil from other sites. The assessment of fill material requires careful characterisation because the contamination can be highly heterogeneous, with hotspots of concentrated contamination within otherwise clean material.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule B1 — Guideline on investigation levels for soil and groundwater; PFAS National Environmental Management Plan (PFAS NEMP) 2020
Applies to: All sites where contamination assessment is required, particularly former industrial, commercial, or agricultural land being developed for sensitive uses
Related standards: Safe Work Australia WES (Workplace Exposure Standards) for asbestos; AS 4964-2004 (Method for the qualitative identification of asbestos in bulk samples)

Common pitfalls:
- Assuming contamination is limited to the contaminant types associated with the most recent land use, when historical uses (which may not be apparent from the current site condition) are often the primary source
- Not considering off-site migration of contaminants, particularly volatile compounds and groundwater-borne contaminants
- Underestimating the prevalence and cost implications of asbestos in soil, which is one of the most commonly encountered contaminants in urban development
- Failing to test for PFAS on sites with any history of firefighting activity, industrial use of PFAS, or proximity to defence facilities or airports

## NEPM Framework and Investigation Levels

The National Environment Protection (Assessment of Site Contamination) Measure 1999, as amended in 2013, is the overarching national framework for contaminated site assessment in Australia. While each state and territory has its own contaminated land legislation, the NEPM provides the nationally consistent methodology, investigation levels, and technical guidance that underpin site assessments across all jurisdictions.

The NEPM establishes a tiered framework for site assessment:

**Tier 1 — Screening assessment:** Comparison of analytical results against published investigation levels. If all results are below the investigation levels for the proposed land use, the site can generally be considered suitable without further assessment. The Tier 1 investigation levels are intentionally conservative (protective) to screen out sites that do not require detailed risk assessment.

**Tier 2 — Site-specific risk assessment:** Where Tier 1 investigation levels are exceeded, a site-specific risk assessment may be conducted to determine whether the contamination actually poses an unacceptable risk, considering site-specific factors such as exposure pathways, receptor sensitivity, and contaminant bioavailability. A Tier 2 assessment may demonstrate that contamination above the generic investigation levels does not pose an unacceptable risk at the specific site.

**Tier 3 — Complex risk assessment:** For complex sites with multiple contaminants, pathways, and receptors, a detailed quantitative risk assessment may be required, potentially including probabilistic modelling and site-specific toxicological assessment.

**Key NEPM investigation levels:**

**Health Investigation Levels (HILs):** Generic soil investigation levels based on protection of human health. The NEPM provides HILs for four land use categories:
- HIL-A: Residential with garden/accessible soil (most conservative)
- HIL-B: Residential with minimal soil access (e.g., apartments with no private garden)
- HIL-C: Public open space (parks, recreational areas)
- HIL-D: Commercial/industrial (least conservative)

**Health Screening Levels (HSLs):** Investigation levels for volatile contaminants that may pose a vapour intrusion risk. HSLs are provided for soil, groundwater, and soil vapour, and are specific to the land use category, soil type, and depth to contamination source.

**Ecological Investigation Levels (EILs):** Investigation levels for protection of terrestrial ecosystems. EILs are derived on a site-specific basis using the NEPM methodology, taking into account background concentrations and soil properties. Urban sites typically apply added contaminant limits (ACLs), while sites with ecological significance may require ambient background-based assessment.

**Groundwater Investigation Levels (GILs):** Investigation levels for groundwater, based on whether the groundwater is used for drinking water, is an aquatic ecosystem receptor, or is a source of vapour intrusion. The NEPM cross-references the ANZECC/ARMCANZ (2000) water quality guidelines (now replaced by the ANZG 2018 guidelines) for ecosystem protection values.

The critical principle in applying investigation levels is land use sensitivity. A site proposed for residential development (HIL-A or HIL-B) has far more conservative investigation levels than the same site proposed for commercial use (HIL-D). This means that a site can be "clean enough" for commercial use but contaminated for residential use. The proposed land use must be clearly established before selecting the applicable investigation levels.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule B1 — Guideline on investigation levels for soil and groundwater; Schedule B4 — Guideline on site-specific health risk assessment
Applies to: All contaminated site assessments in Australia
Related standards: ANZG 2018 (Australian and New Zealand Guidelines for Fresh and Marine Water Quality)

Common pitfalls:
- Applying investigation levels for the current land use rather than the proposed land use — if the site is being developed for a more sensitive use, the more conservative criteria apply
- Treating investigation levels as pass/fail thresholds rather than screening values — exceedance of a Tier 1 investigation level does not automatically mean the site is unsuitable, it means further assessment may be warranted
- Not distinguishing between naturally occurring background concentrations and anthropogenic contamination, particularly for metals which can have elevated natural background levels in certain geological settings
- Applying HIL-A (residential with garden) criteria to an apartment development where HIL-B (residential with minimal soil access) may be appropriate, resulting in unnecessary remediation cost

## State and Territory Regulatory Frameworks

While the NEPM provides the national framework for contaminated site assessment, each state and territory has its own legislation, regulatory bodies, and specific requirements that must be complied with. The key differences between jurisdictions relate to contaminated land registers, site audit requirements, planning triggers, and notification obligations.

**New South Wales (NSW):**
- **Legislation:** Contaminated Land Management Act 1997 (CLM Act); State Environmental Planning Policy (Resilience and Hazards) 2021, Chapter 4 (formerly SEPP 55)
- **Regulator:** NSW Environment Protection Authority (EPA)
- **Key requirements:** SEPP Resilience and Hazards Chapter 4 requires consent authorities to consider contamination for all development applications involving a change of use. The CLM Act requires notification to the EPA where contamination has or may have occurred, and empowers the EPA to issue investigation, remediation, and management orders. NSW has a system of EPA-accredited site auditors who provide independent review of contaminated site assessments.
- **Contaminated sites register:** Maintained by the NSW EPA. Lists sites subject to regulatory action under the CLM Act.

**Victoria (VIC):**
- **Legislation:** Environment Protection Act 2017; Environment Reference Standard (ERS)
- **Regulator:** Environment Protection Authority Victoria (EPA Victoria)
- **Key requirements:** The 2017 Act introduced a general environmental duty (GED) requiring all persons to take reasonably practicable steps to minimise risks of harm to human health and the environment from pollution. The Environment Reference Standard sets environmental quality indicators and objectives. Victoria uses environmental auditors (appointed under the EP Act) to certify site suitability.
- **Contaminated sites register:** Priority Sites Register maintained by EPA Victoria.

**Queensland (QLD):**
- **Legislation:** Environmental Protection Act 1994 (EP Act); Environmental Protection Regulation 2019
- **Regulator:** Department of Environment and Science (DES)
- **Key requirements:** Queensland maintains the Environmental Management Register (EMR) and the Contaminated Land Register (CLR). Sites with notifiable activities (listed in Schedule 3 of the EP Act) are recorded on the EMR. Sites that are contaminated are recorded on the CLR, and a site management plan may be required. Development applications on EMR or CLR listed sites must address contamination.
- **Contaminated sites register:** EMR and CLR maintained by DES.

**Western Australia (WA):**
- **Legislation:** Contaminated Sites Act 2003; Contaminated Sites Regulations 2006
- **Regulator:** Department of Water and Environmental Regulation (DWER)
- **Key requirements:** WA has a mandatory reporting obligation — anyone who knows or suspects that a site is contaminated must report it to DWER. Reported sites are classified on the Contaminated Sites Database. The classification categories range from "not contaminated — unrestricted use" to "contaminated — remediation required". Sites classified as contaminated require remediation or management before development.
- **Contaminated sites register:** Contaminated Sites Database maintained by DWER.

**South Australia (SA):**
- **Legislation:** Environment Protection Act 1993; Environment Protection (Site Contamination) Amendment Act 2007
- **Regulator:** Environment Protection Authority South Australia (EPA SA)
- **Key requirements:** SA has notification obligations for site contamination and a site contamination assessment process that must be completed before certain changes of use. SA also has a system of site contamination auditors who provide independent assessment.
- **Contaminated sites register:** Site contamination index maintained by EPA SA.

**Tasmania (TAS):**
- **Legislation:** Environmental Management and Pollution Control Act 1994 (EMPCA)
- **Regulator:** Department of Natural Resources and Environment (NRE Tas)
- **Key requirements:** Tasmania's approach is less prescriptive than mainland states. Contaminated site issues are typically addressed through the planning system and environmental management conditions.

**Australian Capital Territory (ACT):**
- **Legislation:** Environment Protection Act 1997; Contaminated Sites Environment Protection Policy 2009
- **Regulator:** ACT Environment Protection Authority
- **Key requirements:** The ACT maintains a contaminated sites register and has notification requirements for contamination. Development applications on or near contaminated sites must include environmental assessments.

**Northern Territory (NT):**
- **Legislation:** Waste Management and Pollution Control Act 1998
- **Regulator:** NT Environment Protection Authority
- **Key requirements:** The NT has a contaminated sites register and requires assessment and management of contaminated land as a condition of development. The NT EPA can issue pollution abatement notices for contaminated sites.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule A, Section 1 — Regulatory framework; respective state/territory legislation as listed above
Applies to: All contaminated site assessments — the applicable state/territory legislation determines specific requirements for investigation, remediation, audit, and notification
Related standards: State-specific planning policies and environmental planning instruments

Common pitfalls:
- Assuming the NEPM is the only applicable framework without checking state-specific requirements, which may be more stringent or have different procedural obligations
- Not checking the contaminated sites register in the relevant jurisdiction before purchasing a site — a site on the register may have notification, investigation, or remediation obligations attached
- Failing to notify the relevant EPA where required by legislation — notification obligations vary significantly between jurisdictions and non-compliance can result in penalties
- Not engaging an accredited/appointed site auditor where required by the planning consent or the regulatory framework, leading to delays in obtaining development approval

## Remediation Action Plans (RAPs)

A Remediation Action Plan (RAP) is the engineering and environmental management plan that describes how contamination at a site will be managed or removed to make the site suitable for its proposed use. The RAP is typically prepared by an environmental consultant and may require review by a site auditor and/or regulatory approval before remediation can commence.

A comprehensive RAP should include:

**1. Remediation objectives and criteria:**
Clear statement of the clean-up criteria that must be achieved. These are typically derived from NEPM investigation levels for the proposed land use (Tier 1), or from site-specific risk assessment (Tier 2). The criteria define the measurable endpoints against which remediation success will be assessed.

**2. Remediation methodology:**
Detailed description of the remediation approach, including:
- Excavation volumes and depths
- Treatment technologies to be applied (in-situ or ex-situ)
- Soil disposal or treatment pathway (classification for landfill disposal, treatment for reuse, or on-site management)
- Groundwater management during remediation (dewatering, treatment, discharge)
- Unexpected finds protocol — what actions will be taken if contamination is encountered that differs in nature or extent from what was characterised in the DSI

**3. Environmental management controls:**
- Dust suppression measures (critical for asbestos and fine particulate contamination)
- Noise and vibration management
- Surface water and stormwater management to prevent sediment-laden or contaminated runoff leaving the site
- Odour management for volatile organic compounds
- Traffic management for soil transport vehicles

**4. Health and safety:**
- Occupational health and safety plan for workers on the remediated site
- Personal protective equipment (PPE) requirements
- Air monitoring program during remediation works (particularly for asbestos and volatile compounds)
- Exclusion zones and site security

**5. Validation plan:**
- Sampling strategy to demonstrate that remediation criteria have been achieved
- Number and location of validation samples
- Analytical suite and QA/QC requirements
- Reporting format for the validation report

**6. Contingency measures:**
- What happens if remediation does not achieve the criteria
- Alternative remediation approaches if the primary method is unsuccessful
- Additional investigation requirements if the extent of contamination proves greater than expected

The RAP should be costed and programmed so that the project team can incorporate remediation costs and timeframes into the overall project budget and construction program. Remediation works frequently sit on the critical path because construction cannot commence until the site is validated as suitable.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule A, Section 5 — Site management; NSW EPA guidelines on preparing remediation action plans; state-specific guidelines as applicable
Applies to: Sites where contamination has been confirmed above the applicable criteria for the proposed land use
Related standards: SafeWork Australia Model Code of Practice: How to Safely Remove Asbestos; AS/NZS 2161 (Occupational protective gloves); relevant WHS legislation

Common pitfalls:
- Preparing a RAP before the DSI has adequately delineated the extent and nature of contamination, leading to significant scope changes and cost overruns during remediation
- Not including an unexpected finds protocol, resulting in unplanned work stoppages and emergency decisions when uncharacterised contamination is encountered
- Underestimating the volume of contaminated soil to be excavated — a common source of cost overrun on remediation projects
- Not accounting for groundwater management during excavation, which can be a major cost driver if contaminated groundwater must be treated before discharge
- Failing to engage the site auditor during RAP preparation, leading to auditor objections that require RAP revision and delay

## Remediation Technologies and Approaches

The choice of remediation technology depends on the type of contamination, the extent and concentration, the site geology and hydrogeology, the proposed land use, the project timeframe, and the available budget. Australian remediation practice uses a range of established and emerging technologies.

**Excavation and off-site disposal (dig and dump):**
The most common remediation approach in Australian construction, particularly for smaller sites and where the contamination is localised and accessible. Contaminated soil is excavated, classified for waste disposal, and transported to a licensed landfill. Clean fill is imported to replace the excavated material. Advantages: proven, predictable, and achieves immediate removal of the contamination source. Disadvantages: high cost for large volumes ($100-$400+/tonne including excavation, transport, and disposal levy), limited by landfill capacity and waste acceptance criteria, generates truck traffic and community impact, and does not treat the contamination (merely relocates it).

Waste classification for disposal in NSW follows the EPA's Waste Classification Guidelines (2014), which categorise excavated material as general solid waste, restricted solid waste, or hazardous waste based on contaminant concentrations. Disposal costs increase significantly with waste classification — hazardous waste disposal can cost $300-$600+/tonne compared to $80-$150/tonne for general solid waste (excluding landfill levy). Other states have similar but not identical classification frameworks.

**On-site containment (capping and encapsulation):**
Where contamination is too extensive to excavate economically, or where the contamination is at depth and does not pose a risk under the proposed use with appropriate management, containment may be appropriate. This involves placing a physical barrier (cap) over the contamination to prevent exposure, and may include:
- Clean soil capping layer (minimum 0.5-1.0m of validated clean fill)
- Geotextile separation layer
- Hardstand cover (buildings, paving, concrete slabs)
- Barriers to prevent lateral migration of contaminated groundwater

Containment does not remove the contamination — it manages it in place. This means an ongoing environmental management plan (EMP) is typically required, potentially including groundwater monitoring, cap integrity inspections, and restrictions on future ground disturbance. The site may be listed on the contaminated sites register with a notation that contamination remains under management.

**Bioremediation:**
Uses biological processes (bacteria, fungi, or plants) to break down or transform contaminants. Common applications include:
- Biopiling: excavated soil is placed in above-ground treatment piles with controlled aeration and moisture to promote biodegradation of petroleum hydrocarbons
- Landfarming: contaminated soil is spread in thin layers and periodically tilled to promote aerobic biodegradation
- In-situ bioremediation: injection of nutrients or oxygen into the subsurface to stimulate native microbial populations to degrade contaminants
- Phytoremediation: use of plants to extract, stabilise, or degrade contaminants (typically metals or shallow hydrocarbon contamination)

Bioremediation is most effective for petroleum hydrocarbons and some organic compounds. It is generally not effective for heavy metals or PFAS. Treatment timeframes are typically months to years, which may not align with construction programs.

**Soil vapour extraction (SVE) and air sparging:**
Used for volatile organic compounds (petroleum hydrocarbons, chlorinated solvents) in the vadose (unsaturated) zone. SVE involves applying a vacuum to extraction wells to draw contaminated vapour from the soil for above-ground treatment. Air sparging injects air below the water table to volatilise dissolved contaminants, which are then captured by an SVE system above. These technologies are most effective in permeable sandy soils and less effective in clay or heterogeneous fill.

**Chemical oxidation and reduction (in-situ):**
Injection of chemical reagents into the subsurface to destroy contaminants through oxidation or reduction reactions. Common reagents include:
- Permanganate: effective for chlorinated solvents
- Persulphate (activated): broad-spectrum oxidant for petroleum hydrocarbons and chlorinated solvents
- Hydrogen peroxide (Fenton's reagent): aggressive oxidant for various organic contaminants
- Zero-valent iron (ZVI): reductant for chlorinated solvents and some metals

Chemical treatment can achieve rapid contaminant destruction but requires careful design to manage heat generation, gas production, and potential mobilisation of metals.

**Permeable reactive barriers (PRBs):**
Installed in the path of a contaminated groundwater plume to treat groundwater as it flows through. The barrier material is selected for the target contaminant — ZVI for chlorinated solvents, activated carbon for PFAS, limestone for acid mine drainage. PRBs are a passive technology (no ongoing energy input) but require long-term monitoring and periodic replacement of the reactive material.

**Monitored natural attenuation (MNA):**
Where contamination is present in groundwater but is demonstrably decreasing over time through natural processes (biodegradation, dispersion, sorption), a regulatory authority may accept MNA as the remediation strategy, provided that monitoring demonstrates continued attenuation and that receptors are protected. MNA is not a "do nothing" approach — it requires ongoing groundwater monitoring (typically quarterly for several years) and contingency triggers for active remediation if attenuation stalls.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule A, Section 5; CRC CARE Technical Reports on remediation technologies; NSW EPA guidelines on remediation
Applies to: Sites requiring remediation to achieve suitability for the proposed land use
Related standards: AS 4482.1-2005; state-specific waste classification guidelines for excavation and disposal

Common pitfalls:
- Defaulting to "dig and dump" without evaluating whether in-situ or on-site treatment technologies could achieve the remediation objectives at lower cost and with less disruption
- Underestimating the cost of off-site disposal, particularly waste levies, transport distances, and classification requirements
- Not accounting for the timeframe of bioremediation or MNA approaches when preparing the construction program — these technologies operate on environmental timescales, not construction timescales
- Selecting a remediation technology without considering the site geology (e.g., attempting SVE in clay soils where air flow is severely limited)
- Failing to plan for the management of contaminated groundwater encountered during excavation, which may require on-site treatment and licensed discharge

## Validation and Verification

Validation is the process of demonstrating, through sampling and analysis, that remediation has achieved the agreed clean-up criteria and that the site is suitable for its proposed use. The validation report is a critical document that provides the evidentiary basis for certifying that the remediation objectives have been met.

**Validation sampling requirements:**

The number, location, and depth of validation samples must be sufficient to demonstrate that the remediation criteria have been achieved across the entire remediated area. The general principles are:

- **Base of excavation:** Samples are collected from the base and sidewalls of excavations to confirm that contamination above the criteria has been removed. A minimum sampling density of 1 sample per 25 m2 of excavation base is commonly applied for small to medium excavations, with reduced density for larger areas where contamination is uniform.
- **Imported fill:** All imported clean fill material must be validated before or upon receipt to confirm it is free of contamination. Testing frequency depends on the source — fill from a known clean source (e.g., quarry) requires less testing than fill from an unknown or recycled source.
- **Capping layer:** Where remediation involves capping, the clean fill capping material must be validated to confirm it meets the criteria for the proposed land use.
- **Groundwater:** Post-remediation groundwater monitoring is typically required for at least two to four quarterly rounds to demonstrate that groundwater quality meets the applicable criteria and that the concentration trend is stable or declining.

**Validation reporting:**

A validation report should include:
- Description of the remediation works undertaken, including volumes excavated, disposal destinations, and treatment methods
- Validation sampling methodology, including sample locations shown on scaled plans
- Laboratory analytical results with comparison against the applicable criteria
- QA/QC results demonstrating data quality
- Updated conceptual site model showing the post-remediation condition
- Conclusions on whether the remediation criteria have been achieved
- Any residual contamination and recommended ongoing management measures

**Waste tracking:**
All contaminated material removed from the site must be tracked from source to disposal destination using waste tracking documentation as required by the relevant state legislation. In NSW, this is the WasteLocate system; other states have equivalent tracking systems. Waste tracking provides a chain of custody demonstrating that contaminated material has been lawfully disposed of.

Reference: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013, Schedule A, Section 4 — Data collection, sample design and reporting; NSW EPA guidelines on validation
Applies to: All sites where remediation has been undertaken
Related standards: AS 4482.1-2005 (sampling guidance); state-specific waste tracking requirements

Common pitfalls:
- Insufficient validation sampling — taking too few samples to demonstrate the remediation criteria have been achieved, leading to the site auditor requiring additional sampling
- Not validating imported fill material, creating the risk that contaminated fill is placed on a remediated site (this occurs more often than expected)
- Not maintaining waste tracking documentation, which can result in penalties and undermine the validation of the remediation
- Delaying the validation report, which delays the site audit and ultimately the development approval
- Failing to include post-remediation groundwater monitoring in the validation plan, which may be required by the site auditor or regulator

## Site Audit and the Auditor Role

In several Australian jurisdictions, a site audit by an accredited or appointed site auditor is required before a contaminated site can be certified as suitable for its proposed use. The site auditor provides independent expert review of the environmental assessment and remediation work, and issues a formal site audit statement (SAS) and site audit report (SAR) that certify whether the site is suitable.

**When is a site audit required?**

Site audits are commonly required in the following circumstances:
- Development consent conditions requiring a site audit before construction or occupation
- Voluntary remediation where the proponent seeks formal confirmation of site suitability
- Regulatory orders under contaminated land legislation
- When contamination exceeds the Tier 1 investigation levels and a site-specific risk assessment has been undertaken (the risk assessment typically requires auditor review)
- When remediation has been undertaken and validation needs independent verification

**Types of site audit statements:**

**Section A (or equivalent):** The auditor certifies that the site investigation has been undertaken in accordance with the NEPM and that the site can be made suitable for the proposed use, subject to completion of remediation/management works described in an approved RAP. This is typically issued before remediation commences.

**Section B (or equivalent):** The auditor certifies that the site has been remediated and is suitable for the proposed use, either unconditionally or subject to conditions (e.g., ongoing groundwater monitoring, cap maintenance, land use restrictions). This is issued after remediation and validation are complete.

**Auditor independence:**
The site auditor must be independent of the consultant who prepared the environmental reports. This independence requirement ensures that the audit provides a genuine check on the quality and adequacy of the assessment and remediation work. The auditor is engaged by the site owner but has a statutory obligation to act independently and in accordance with professional standards.

**Cost and timing:**
Site audit fees typically range from $15,000 to $80,000 or more, depending on the complexity of the site, the volume of documentation to be reviewed, and the number of site inspections required. The audit process can take 3-6 months, and environmental consultants should engage the auditor early in the assessment process to avoid surprises and delays.

Reference: NSW CLM Act 1997, Part 4 — Site audits; VIC EP Act 2017, Part 8.3 — Environmental audits; respective state/territory legislation for auditor appointment and accreditation
Applies to: Sites where a site audit is required by planning consent, regulatory order, or best practice
Related standards: NEPM (Assessment of Site Contamination) 2013

Common pitfalls:
- Not engaging the site auditor early enough — auditors who are brought in at the validation stage may identify deficiencies in the investigation or remediation that require additional work, causing delays
- Not providing the auditor with complete documentation (all investigation reports, laboratory certificates, waste tracking records, and validation data), leading to requests for information that delay the audit
- Selecting the cheapest auditor without checking their experience with the specific contamination type and jurisdiction, which can lead to overly conservative or impractical conditions on the audit statement
- Assuming a "suitable with conditions" audit statement means the conditions are minor — conditions such as ongoing groundwater monitoring or cap maintenance can impose significant long-term cost and management obligations

## Asbestos Assessment and Management in Construction

Asbestos is one of the most commonly encountered contaminants in Australian construction, particularly on sites involving demolition, refurbishment, or development on land where previous buildings have been demolished. The regulatory framework for asbestos is extensive and the health consequences of improper management are severe.

**Asbestos in buildings:**

Asbestos-containing materials (ACM) were widely used in Australian construction from the 1940s to the mid-1980s, and some products containing asbestos continued to be used until 2003. Common locations include:
- Flat and corrugated cement sheeting (fibro) — walls, ceilings, eaves
- Vinyl floor tiles and adhesive
- Pipe insulation and lagging
- Electrical switchboard backing
- Roof shingles and tiles
- Brake linings in plant and equipment
- Fire doors and fire-rated walls
- Gaskets and seals in mechanical plant

Before any demolition or refurbishment of a building constructed (or suspected to have been constructed) before 2003, an asbestos survey must be conducted by a competent person. The survey identifies the location, type, condition, and quantity of all ACM in the building. This survey informs the asbestos removal plan and is a legal requirement under WHS legislation.

**Asbestos in soil:**

Asbestos in soil is assessed under the NEPM framework, which provides specific guidance in Schedule B1. The assessment considers:
- The quantity of asbestos in soil (weight percentage of fibrous asbestos and asbestos fines)
- The form of asbestos (bonded/non-friable vs friable)
- The potential for disturbance and fibre release based on the proposed land use
- The depth of asbestos-containing material relative to the surface

Investigation levels for asbestos in soil are:
- Bonded ACM: No visible ACM or AF on the surface (for all land uses)
- Fibrous asbestos (FA) and asbestos fines (AF) in soil: 0.001% w/w FA or AF for residential and public open space; 0.01% w/w for commercial/industrial (with appropriate management controls)

**Asbestos removal:**

Asbestos removal must be carried out by licensed asbestos removalists:
- **Class A licence:** Required for friable asbestos removal (highest risk)
- **Class B licence:** Required for bonded (non-friable) asbestos removal exceeding 10 m2

Air monitoring during asbestos removal must be conducted by a licensed asbestos assessor (independent of the removalist). Clearance inspections and air monitoring results must demonstrate that respirable asbestos fibre concentrations are below 0.01 fibres/mL before the area can be reoccupied.

The cost of asbestos removal varies significantly depending on the type (friable vs bonded), location, accessibility, and volume. Indicative costs for professional removal:
- Bonded asbestos sheeting removal: $30-$80/m2
- Friable asbestos removal (with Class A containment): $200-$600+/m2
- Asbestos-contaminated soil excavation and disposal: $250-$500+/tonne (including specialist handling, transport, and licensed disposal)

Reference: SafeWork Australia Model Code of Practice: How to Manage and Control Asbestos in the Workplace; SafeWork Australia Model Code of Practice: How to Safely Remove Asbestos; NEPM 2013 Schedule B1 (asbestos in soil investigation levels); WHS Regulations 2017 (Chapter 8 — Asbestos)
Applies to: All demolition, refurbishment, and construction projects on sites where ACM may be present
Related standards: AS 4964-2004 (qualitative identification of asbestos); NOHSC:3003(2005) (guidance on the membrane filter method for estimating airborne asbestos fibres)

Common pitfalls:
- Not conducting an asbestos survey before demolition or refurbishment, resulting in uncontrolled asbestos disturbance and potential worker exposure
- Assuming that buildings constructed after 1990 are asbestos-free — ACM was used in some products until 2003
- Underestimating the cost and time required for asbestos removal, particularly friable asbestos which requires Class A removal under strict containment
- Not testing soil for asbestos on sites where previous buildings have been demolished — demolition debris containing ACM is frequently found in fill material
- Failing to maintain an asbestos register for existing buildings, which is a legal requirement under WHS legislation

## PFAS Contamination — Assessment and Management

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) have emerged as one of the most significant environmental contamination challenges in Australia. PFAS contamination can have profound implications for property development, land values, and project feasibility, and the regulatory framework is still evolving.

**Key characteristics of PFAS:**
- Extremely persistent in the environment — they do not break down under normal environmental conditions
- Highly mobile in water — PFAS contamination plumes in groundwater can extend for kilometres from the source
- Bio-accumulative — they concentrate in biological tissues over time
- Found in very low concentrations that can still trigger regulatory action (micrograms per litre in water, micrograms per kilogram in soil)

**Common PFAS sources in Australian development:**
- Defence bases (firefighting training with AFFF)
- Civilian airports (firefighting foam use)
- Fire training facilities
- Industrial sites where PFAS was used in manufacturing (textiles, non-stick coatings, food packaging)
- Waste facilities that received PFAS-containing waste
- Wastewater treatment plant biosolids applied to land

**PFAS National Environmental Management Plan (PFAS NEMP):**

The PFAS NEMP (updated 2020) provides the national guidance framework for PFAS assessment and management. Key elements include:

- **Human health screening criteria:** The PFAS NEMP adopts criteria for PFOS, PFOA, and PFHxS (individually and as a sum) in soil and groundwater, differentiated by land use (residential, commercial/industrial, recreational)
- **Ecological screening criteria:** Criteria for protection of aquatic ecosystems and terrestrial ecosystems
- **Drinking water criteria:** Based on Australian Drinking Water Guidelines (ADWG) values for PFOS and PFOA
- **Precautionary approach:** Given scientific uncertainty, the PFAS NEMP recommends a precautionary approach and notes that criteria may be revised as scientific understanding evolves

**PFAS remediation challenges:**

PFAS contamination is uniquely challenging to remediate because:
- Conventional remediation technologies (bioremediation, chemical oxidation) are largely ineffective against PFAS due to the strength of the carbon-fluorine bond
- Excavation and disposal options are limited because most landfills do not accept PFAS-contaminated waste, and high-temperature incineration (>1,000°C) is required for destruction
- Groundwater treatment typically uses granular activated carbon (GAC) or ion exchange resin, which adsorb PFAS from water but require ongoing replacement and disposal of spent media
- Emerging technologies include high-temperature thermal treatment, electrochemical oxidation, and supercritical water oxidation, but these are not yet widely commercially available in Australia

**Implications for property development:**

PFAS contamination can significantly impact property values and project feasibility:
- Remediation costs can be very high and uncertain
- Ongoing management and monitoring obligations may be attached to the site
- Community and regulatory sensitivity to PFAS can affect development approvals
- Financing and insurance may be difficult to obtain for PFAS-affected sites
- Due diligence for any site near a known or potential PFAS source should include PFAS-specific investigation

Reference: PFAS National Environmental Management Plan (PFAS NEMP) 2020; HEPA PFAS Guidelines; NEPM 2013 (as supplemented by PFAS NEMP)
Applies to: Sites with known or suspected PFAS contamination, and sites near PFAS sources
Related standards: Australian Drinking Water Guidelines (ADWG); ANZG 2018 water quality guidelines

Common pitfalls:
- Not investigating for PFAS on sites near airports, defence bases, or fire training areas, where PFAS contamination from AFFF is common
- Underestimating the extent of PFAS groundwater plumes, which can travel much further from the source than conventional contaminants
- Applying remediation cost estimates based on conventional contamination to PFAS projects — PFAS remediation is typically more expensive and takes longer
- Not considering the impact of PFAS contamination on project financing, insurance, and future property value
- Failing to monitor evolving PFAS regulatory criteria, which may become more stringent as scientific understanding develops

## Cost Management for Remediation Projects

Remediation projects are inherently more uncertain than standard construction projects because the full extent of contamination may not be known until remediation is underway. This uncertainty has profound implications for cost planning, budgeting, and contract strategy. The quantity surveyor's role on a remediation project requires a different mindset — one that embraces uncertainty and builds robust contingency frameworks.

**Cost estimation challenges:**

The key challenges in remediation cost estimation include:
- **Unknown contamination extent:** Even a thorough DSI provides a statistical estimate of contamination distribution, not a definitive survey. The actual volume of contaminated material encountered during excavation frequently exceeds the estimated volume by 20-50% or more.
- **Waste classification uncertainty:** The waste classification of excavated material (which determines disposal cost) may differ from what was expected based on pre-remediation sampling. A change in classification from general solid waste to restricted or hazardous waste can double or triple disposal costs per tonne.
- **Groundwater management:** The volume and quality of groundwater encountered during excavation is often difficult to predict accurately. Treatment requirements and discharge approvals add cost and time.
- **Unexpected finds:** Historical sites frequently yield unexpected contamination types, buried structures (foundations, tanks, pipes), or cultural heritage items that require unplanned investigation and management.

**Typical remediation cost ranges (indicative, as of 2024-2025):**

- Minor soil contamination (localised, accessible): $50,000-$200,000
- Moderate soil contamination (underground storage tank removal and soil remediation): $200,000-$1,000,000
- Major soil remediation (widespread contamination, large excavation volumes): $1,000,000-$10,000,000+
- Groundwater remediation (pump and treat, PRB, or in-situ treatment): $500,000-$5,000,000+ (often over multiple years)
- Asbestos removal from soil (large-scale): $500,000-$5,000,000+
- PFAS remediation: highly variable, typically $1,000,000-$20,000,000+ depending on extent

**Contingency for remediation projects:**

The design document's cost management principles note that remediation projects warrant 20-30% contingency on estimated remediation costs. This is significantly higher than standard construction contingency (5-10%) and reflects the inherent uncertainty in contamination extent, waste classification, and unexpected finds. For poorly characterised sites (limited DSI data), contingency of 30-50% may be appropriate.

**Contract strategy for remediation:**

The choice of contract form for remediation works requires careful consideration of risk allocation:
- **Lump sum:** Appropriate only where contamination is well-characterised and the remediation scope is clearly defined. The contractor bears the volume risk, which will be priced into the tender.
- **Schedule of rates:** More common for remediation works. The principal bears the volume risk, but unit rates are fixed. This is appropriate where the remediation methodology is defined but the quantities are uncertain.
- **Cost-plus:** Used where the scope is highly uncertain and cannot be reliably quantified at tender. The principal bears all cost risk. Requires robust cost monitoring and audit provisions.
- **Target cost / pain-share gain-share:** A hybrid approach where both parties share in cost outcomes above or below the target. This incentivises efficiency while sharing the risk of uncertain scope.

Reference: AIQS Cost Management Practice Guide (remediation-specific considerations); NSW EPA guidelines on remediation; Contaminated Land Management Act 1997 (NSW) cost recovery provisions
Applies to: All remediation projects
Related standards: AS 4000-1997 (with amendments for remediation works); relevant WHS legislation for hazardous materials handling

Common pitfalls:
- Estimating remediation costs based solely on unit rates without adequately assessing the likely volume of contaminated material — volume risk is the primary cost driver
- Not including sufficient contingency for unknown contamination, particularly on sites with limited investigation data
- Using a lump sum contract for remediation works where the contamination extent is poorly characterised, creating contractual disputes when actual volumes exceed estimates
- Failing to separately budget for ancillary costs (traffic management, dust suppression, groundwater treatment, site auditor fees, environmental monitoring) which can represent 20-40% of the total remediation cost
- Not including ongoing monitoring and management costs in the project budget — post-remediation monitoring can extend for 5-10 years or more

## Due Diligence Reporting for Property Transactions

Environmental due diligence reports are prepared to inform property transactions, particularly where the purchaser, lender, or insurer needs assurance about the contamination status and environmental liabilities associated with a site. The scope and depth of due diligence depends on the transaction type, the site history, and the risk appetite of the parties.

**Types of due diligence reports:**

**Desktop due diligence review:** A high-level review of readily available information (contaminated sites registers, historical land use records, previous environmental reports, aerial photographs) to identify red flags. This is the minimum level of due diligence and is often sufficient for low-risk sites with no contamination history. Cost: typically $3,000-$8,000.

**Phase 1 Environmental Site Assessment:** A comprehensive desktop review and site inspection as described in the Preliminary Site Assessment section of this guide. Provides a detailed assessment of contamination risk without intrusive sampling. Cost: typically $8,000-$25,000 depending on site size and complexity.

**Phase 2 Environmental Site Assessment:** Includes intrusive investigation and laboratory analysis. Required where the Phase 1 identifies contamination risk. Cost: typically $20,000-$100,000+ depending on the number of samples, analytical suite, and whether groundwater wells are installed.

**Quantified contamination liability estimate:** Where contamination is confirmed, an estimate of the remediation cost, timeframe, and ongoing management obligations. This is used to inform the purchase price adjustment or environmental escrow arrangements. The estimate should present a range (low-medium-high) reflecting the uncertainty inherent in remediation cost estimation.

**Transaction risk allocation mechanisms:**

- **Purchase price adjustment:** The remediation cost estimate is deducted from the agreed purchase price. The purchaser assumes the remediation risk and responsibility.
- **Environmental escrow:** A portion of the purchase price is held in escrow until remediation is completed and validated. The escrow amount typically includes a contingency buffer above the central remediation cost estimate.
- **Environmental indemnity:** The vendor provides an indemnity to the purchaser for contamination liabilities arising from pre-acquisition activities. The enforceability and practical value of this indemnity depends on the vendor's ongoing financial capacity.
- **Environmental insurance:** Pollution legal liability insurance can transfer some or all of the contamination risk to an insurer. Coverage typically includes known contamination (where the remediation cost may exceed estimates) and unknown contamination (contamination not identified in the due diligence investigation). Premiums are typically 3-8% of the policy limit.

Reference: NSW CLM Act 1997 (liability provisions); NEPM 2013; Australian Property Institute due diligence guidance
Applies to: All property transactions involving sites with known or potential contamination
Related standards: API Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct; relevant state contaminated land legislation

Common pitfalls:
- Relying on a desktop review alone for a site with a potentially contaminating history, without commissioning a Phase 1 or Phase 2 investigation
- Not checking the contaminated sites register in the relevant state/territory before completing the transaction
- Accepting the vendor's environmental reports at face value without independent review by a qualified environmental consultant
- Underestimating the remediation cost allowance in the purchase price adjustment, particularly for contamination types with high cost uncertainty (asbestos in soil, PFAS, groundwater contamination)
- Not securing an appropriate risk allocation mechanism (escrow, indemnity, or insurance) for contamination liabilities, leaving the purchaser exposed to unforeseen remediation costs

## Environmental Management Plans

An Environmental Management Plan (EMP) is a management framework document that describes how environmental risks will be managed during construction and/or over the long term on a remediated site. EMPs are commonly required as conditions of development consent, remediation approval, or site audit statements.

**Construction Environmental Management Plan (CEMP):**

A CEMP governs environmental management during construction works. For sites with contamination or sensitive environmental conditions, the CEMP should address:

- **Soil and groundwater management:** Procedures for identifying, handling, and disposing of contaminated material encountered during construction. This includes unexpected finds protocols, soil stockpile management, and groundwater treatment requirements.
- **Erosion and sediment control:** Measures to prevent sediment-laden runoff from leaving the site and entering waterways. This typically follows the "Blue Book" (Managing Urban Stormwater: Soils and Construction, Volume 1, Landcom 2004) principles.
- **Dust management:** Controls to minimise dust generation, particularly important where asbestos or other hazardous particulates may be present. Measures include water suppression, speed limits, sealed access roads, and air monitoring.
- **Noise and vibration:** Compliance with construction noise guidelines (NSW Interim Construction Noise Guideline or equivalent) and vibration limits for adjacent structures.
- **Waste management:** Classification, tracking, and lawful disposal of all waste materials generated during construction.
- **Acid sulfate soils (ASS):** Where the site is identified as having acid sulfate soil risk (common in coastal and estuarine areas of eastern Australia), the CEMP must include an ASS management plan addressing excavation, neutralisation (typically with agricultural lime), and testing requirements.
- **Heritage and cultural considerations:** Protocols for managing unexpected archaeological or Aboriginal cultural heritage finds.

**Long-Term Environmental Management Plan (LTEMP):**

Where a site audit statement includes conditions for ongoing management of residual contamination, an LTEMP is required. This typically includes:
- Groundwater monitoring schedule (sampling locations, frequency, analytes, and trigger levels)
- Cap integrity inspections (for capped sites) and maintenance requirements
- Land use restrictions and notifications to future occupants/owners
- Reporting requirements to the relevant EPA or site auditor
- Contingency actions if trigger levels are exceeded

The LTEMP creates ongoing cost obligations that must be factored into the project budget and disclosed in property transactions. A groundwater monitoring program can cost $15,000-$50,000 per annum depending on the number of wells, sampling frequency, and analytical requirements.

Reference: NSW EPA guidelines on environmental management plans; NSW Interim Construction Noise Guideline; Landcom "Blue Book" (Managing Urban Stormwater: Soils and Construction)
Applies to: All construction projects on contaminated or environmentally sensitive sites
Related standards: AS/NZS ISO 14001 (Environmental management systems); relevant state planning consent conditions

Common pitfalls:
- Preparing a generic CEMP that does not address the site-specific contamination risks identified in the environmental investigation, resulting in inadequate controls during construction
- Not including an unexpected finds protocol in the CEMP, leading to unplanned work stoppages when contamination is encountered during excavation
- Underestimating the long-term cost of environmental management obligations under an LTEMP, particularly groundwater monitoring which can continue for 10-30 years
- Not disclosing LTEMP obligations to future purchasers of the property, which can constitute a breach of the vendor's disclosure obligations

## Acid Sulfate Soils

Acid sulfate soils (ASS) are naturally occurring soils and sediments that contain iron sulfides (predominantly pyrite). When exposed to air through excavation, dewatering, or lowering of the water table, the sulfides oxidise to produce sulfuric acid. This acid can leach metals from the soil, contaminate waterways, corrode building materials, and cause ecological damage. ASS are one of the most commonly encountered environmental constraints in coastal and estuarine areas of eastern Australia.

**Distribution:**
ASS are found extensively along the Australian coastline, particularly in:
- Coastal floodplains and estuarine environments (very common in NSW, QLD, and northern Australia)
- Mangrove areas, tidal flats, and reclaimed land
- Former lake and lagoon beds
- Some inland areas with sulfide-bearing geological formations

State planning instruments typically include ASS risk maps that identify areas where ASS may be present. In NSW, the Acid Sulfate Soils Planning Maps classify land into five risk classes based on the likelihood and depth of ASS occurrence.

**Assessment:**

ASS assessment involves:
1. Desktop review of ASS risk maps and site geological setting
2. Field investigation with soil sampling at specified intervals through the soil profile
3. Laboratory analysis — the standard test method is the Chromium Reducible Sulfur (SCR) test, which measures both existing acidity (Titratable Actual Acidity — TAA) and potential acidity (sulfidic content that will produce acid if oxidised)
4. Calculation of the liming rate — the quantity of agricultural lime (calcium carbonate) required to neutralise the acid produced if the soil is disturbed

**Management during construction:**

If ASS are encountered during construction, the management strategy typically involves:
- Minimising the disturbance of ASS (avoid excavating or dewatering below the water table where possible)
- Treating excavated ASS with agricultural lime at the rate determined by laboratory analysis (typically 5-30 kg lime per tonne of soil)
- Monitoring treatment effectiveness through post-treatment pH and peroxide oxidation testing
- Managing drainage and dewatering to prevent acidic water entering waterways
- Disposing of untreatable ASS at a licensed facility

**Impact on construction costs:**

ASS management can add significant cost to a project, particularly where deep excavation or extensive dewatering is required. Indicative costs include:
- ASS investigation and assessment: $5,000-$30,000
- Lime treatment of excavated soils: $15-$40/tonne (including lime supply and mixing)
- Neutralisation monitoring and testing: $5,000-$20,000 per project
- Water treatment for acidic groundwater: $20,000-$100,000+ depending on volume and treatment requirements

Reference: NSW Acid Sulfate Soils Manual (Acid Sulfate Soils Management Advisory Committee — ASSMAC, 1998); Queensland Acid Sulfate Soils Technical Manual; Dear et al. (2014) NAGD guidance
Applies to: All construction projects in areas identified as having ASS risk, particularly coastal and estuarine areas of NSW, QLD, VIC, SA, WA, and NT
Related standards: State planning maps for ASS risk classification; Landcom Blue Book

Common pitfalls:
- Not investigating for ASS in areas identified on state planning maps, leading to unplanned costs when ASS are encountered during excavation
- Underestimating the volume of lime required for neutralisation, resulting in insufficiently treated soil that continues to produce acid
- Not monitoring dewatering discharge for acidity, allowing acidic water to enter stormwater systems and waterways
- Failing to include ASS management costs in the project budget, despite the site being located in a mapped ASS risk area

## Stakeholder and Regulatory Engagement

Remediation projects involve a broader range of regulatory and community stakeholders than standard construction projects. Effective stakeholder engagement is essential for obtaining approvals, managing expectations, and avoiding delays.

**Key stakeholders in remediation projects:**

**Environmental Protection Authority (EPA):**
The EPA in each state is the primary environmental regulator. The EPA may be involved through:
- Site contamination notification requirements
- Review and approval of remediation action plans (in some jurisdictions)
- Issuing investigation or remediation orders on contaminated sites
- Maintaining the contaminated sites register
- Compliance monitoring and enforcement

Engaging proactively with the EPA is strongly recommended, particularly for complex or high-profile sites. Early engagement can help identify regulatory expectations, avoid unexpected requirements, and build a constructive relationship that facilitates approvals.

**Local council:**
Council's involvement in contamination assessment is typically through the development assessment process. Councils may:
- Require contamination assessment as a condition of development consent (triggered by SEPP or equivalent planning controls)
- Refer applications on contaminated sites to the EPA for comment
- Impose conditions relating to remediation, validation, and ongoing management
- Request submission of environmental management plans before construction commences

**Site auditors:**
As described in the Site Audit section, accredited site auditors provide independent expert review. Early engagement with the auditor is critical — auditors who are consulted during the investigation and remediation planning phase can provide guidance that prevents costly rework.

**Community:**
Contamination and remediation activities can generate significant community concern, particularly in residential areas. Community engagement strategies should include:
- Clear, non-technical communication about the nature of contamination and the remediation approach
- Notification of remediation works, including expected duration, traffic impacts, and environmental controls
- A complaints and enquiries mechanism with prompt response procedures
- Regular progress updates throughout the remediation program

**Adjoining property owners:**
Where contamination has migrated (or may have migrated) onto adjacent properties through groundwater or soil vapour, adjoining owners must be consulted. This is both a regulatory requirement in some jurisdictions and a practical necessity for managing off-site investigation and remediation access.

Reference: State EPA consultation guidelines; Local Government Act provisions; respective contaminated land legislation
Applies to: All remediation projects and contaminated site assessments
Related standards: NEPM 2013 Schedule A (stakeholder engagement principles)

Common pitfalls:
- Not notifying the EPA where required by legislation, which can result in penalties and damage the relationship with the regulator
- Leaving community engagement until complaints are received, rather than proactively communicating before remediation works commence
- Not engaging the site auditor early enough, leading to the auditor identifying issues with the investigation approach after significant work has been completed
- Failing to communicate remediation timelines and potential disruptions to adjacent property owners, resulting in complaints and potential legal action
- Not maintaining records of stakeholder communication, which may be required to demonstrate compliance with consent conditions

## Common Remediation Scenarios in Australian Construction

This section describes typical contamination scenarios encountered in Australian construction projects, their characteristics, and general management approaches. These scenarios are intended to help project managers and quantity surveyors recognise contamination risks and understand the likely cost and time implications.

**Scenario 1: Former service station site**

One of the most common remediation scenarios in urban development. The contamination profile typically includes petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH, BTEX) in soil and potentially in groundwater from leaking underground storage tanks (USTs). Management involves:
- Removal of USTs and associated infrastructure (bowsers, pipework, interceptor pits)
- Excavation of petroleum-contaminated soil around and beneath tank locations
- Assessment and potential treatment of dissolved-phase groundwater contamination
- Soil vapour assessment for BTEX if buildings are proposed
- Typical remediation cost: $200,000-$2,000,000 depending on the number of tanks, extent of leakage, and whether groundwater is affected

**Scenario 2: Former industrial site redeveloped for residential use**

Change of use from industrial to residential triggers the most conservative assessment criteria (HIL-A). Common contaminants include heavy metals, petroleum hydrocarbons, asbestos in soil, and potentially chlorinated solvents depending on the industrial history. Management involves:
- Comprehensive Phase 2 investigation covering all potential contaminant types
- Remediation to achieve HIL-A (or HIL-B for apartments) criteria
- Often involves extensive excavation and replacement with validated clean fill
- Site audit typically required before residential occupation
- Typical remediation cost: $500,000-$10,000,000+ depending on site area and contamination extent

**Scenario 3: Demolition site with asbestos in soil**

When a building containing asbestos is demolished (particularly if demolition occurred before modern environmental controls were standard), asbestos fragments and fines can be distributed through the soil. Management involves:
- Asbestos-in-soil investigation following NEPM guidance
- Excavation and disposal of asbestos-contaminated soil by Class A licensed removalists (if friable asbestos) or Class B (if bonded only)
- Emplacement and validation of clean fill capping layer
- Air monitoring during all excavation works
- Waste tracking for all asbestos waste transported off-site
- Typical remediation cost: $100,000-$5,000,000+ depending on the extent of asbestos distribution and whether friable asbestos is present

**Scenario 4: Coastal development with acid sulfate soils**

Developments in coastal and estuarine areas frequently encounter ASS. While not strictly contamination, ASS management is integral to environmental compliance. Management involves:
- ASS investigation and liming rate calculation
- Lime treatment of excavated soils
- Management of dewatering discharge (pH monitoring and treatment)
- ASS management plan as part of the CEMP
- Typical additional cost: $50,000-$500,000 depending on excavation volumes and dewatering requirements

**Scenario 5: Site adjacent to PFAS source**

Development on or near a site affected by PFAS contamination from a nearby source (airport, defence base, fire training area). Management involves:
- PFAS-specific investigation of soil, groundwater, and potentially bore water and surface water
- Assessment against PFAS NEMP criteria for the proposed land use
- Potential groundwater treatment if drinking water or ecological receptors are affected
- Engagement with the PFAS source owner regarding liability and cost-sharing
- Possible requirement for ongoing groundwater monitoring and management
- Typical assessment and initial management cost: $100,000-$1,000,000+; long-term groundwater treatment: $500,000-$20,000,000+

Reference: NSW EPA contaminated sites information; various state EPA case studies and guidance materials
Applies to: All development projects on or adjacent to potentially contaminated land
Related standards: NEPM 2013; PFAS NEMP 2020; state-specific contaminated land legislation

Common pitfalls:
- Assuming a site is clean because the current use appears benign — historical uses may have left a legacy of contamination
- Not conducting sufficient due diligence before committing to a site acquisition or development program
- Underestimating the cost and time implications of remediation, particularly for groundwater contamination and PFAS, which can extend the remediation program by years
- Not including remediation costs in the project feasibility assessment, leading to projects that are financially unviable once the true environmental liability is understood
- Failing to engage specialist environmental consultants early in the project, relying instead on general construction knowledge to assess contamination risks

## Regulatory Approvals and Planning Controls

Remediation and development of contaminated land requires navigating a complex approval pathway that involves both planning and environmental legislation. Understanding these controls is essential for realistic project programming.

**Planning triggers for contamination assessment:**

In NSW, SEPP (Resilience and Hazards) 2021, Chapter 4 (formerly SEPP 55) requires the consent authority to consider whether land is contaminated before granting consent for any development involving a change of use. The consent authority must be satisfied that the land is suitable (or will be made suitable) for the proposed use before granting consent. This typically requires the applicant to submit environmental assessment reports with the development application.

Other states have equivalent planning triggers:
- Victoria: Ministerial Direction No. 1 (Potentially Contaminated Land) under the Planning and Environment Act 1987
- Queensland: State Planning Policy — natural hazards, risk and resilience (contaminated land provisions)
- WA: Department of Planning guidelines on contaminated sites in planning
- SA: Environment Protection (Site Contamination) Amendment Act 2007 provisions

**Sequencing of approvals:**

The typical approval sequence for development on contaminated land is:

1. **Phase 1 and Phase 2 assessments** → submitted with development application (DA)
2. **DA determination** → consent granted with conditions requiring remediation, validation, and site audit before construction or occupation
3. **RAP preparation** → reviewed by site auditor, who issues a Section A site audit statement confirming the site can be made suitable
4. **Remediation works** → carried out in accordance with the approved RAP
5. **Validation** → validation report prepared demonstrating remediation criteria achieved
6. **Site audit** → auditor reviews validation report and issues a Section B site audit statement confirming the site is suitable
7. **Construction consent conditions satisfied** → construction (beyond remediation works) can commence or occupation can occur

This sequence can take 6-24 months or more from initial assessment to site audit clearance, depending on the complexity of contamination and the regulatory pathway. This timeframe must be factored into the overall project program.

Reference: SEPP (Resilience and Hazards) 2021, Chapter 4 (NSW); respective state planning legislation and policies; Environmental Planning and Assessment Act 1979 (NSW)
Applies to: All development projects on contaminated or potentially contaminated land
Related standards: State-specific development assessment procedures

Common pitfalls:
- Not submitting environmental reports with the development application, leading to delays when the consent authority requires contamination assessment as a condition
- Underestimating the time required for the site audit process, which can take 3-6 months after the validation report is submitted
- Assuming remediation can be concurrent with construction — in many cases, the site must be validated and audited before construction activities (beyond remediation) can commence
- Not engaging with the consent authority early to clarify the contamination assessment requirements, which can differ between councils even within the same state

## Long-Term Site Management and Monitoring

Where remediation does not completely remove all contamination — which is common for large-scale contamination, deep contamination, or contaminants that are difficult to remediate (PFAS, DNAPL) — ongoing management and monitoring may be required for many years. These long-term obligations have significant cost and administrative implications that must be understood and budgeted from the outset.

**Ongoing groundwater monitoring:**

Post-remediation groundwater monitoring is the most common long-term management obligation. The monitoring program typically includes:
- Quarterly sampling of monitoring wells (may reduce to six-monthly or annual once stable trends are established)
- Laboratory analysis for the contaminants of concern
- Assessment of groundwater levels and flow direction
- Comparison of results against trigger levels set in the site audit statement or LTEMP
- Annual monitoring reports submitted to the EPA or site auditor

Costs: $15,000-$50,000 per annum for a typical program with 4-10 monitoring wells sampled quarterly. Over a 10-year monitoring period, the total cost can reach $150,000-$500,000.

**Cap maintenance and integrity monitoring:**

Where contamination has been capped (covered with a barrier layer), ongoing inspections are required to confirm cap integrity:
- Annual or biannual visual inspections of the cap surface
- Monitoring for settlement, cracking, erosion, or vegetation root penetration
- Repair of any identified deficiencies
- Record-keeping of inspection results

**Institutional controls:**

In some cases, the site audit statement may include conditions that restrict future land use or impose ongoing obligations:
- Restrictions on excavation below a specified depth (to prevent disturbance of capped contamination)
- Requirements for environmental management protocols during any future ground disturbance
- Notations on the title or planning records advising of residual contamination
- Requirements for specific building design features (e.g., sub-floor ventilation systems for vapour intrusion management)

These institutional controls must be disclosed in any future property transaction and maintained by successive owners.

**Monitoring well maintenance:**

Groundwater monitoring wells are physical infrastructure that requires maintenance:
- Regular development (purging) to maintain well performance
- Repair or replacement of damaged wells
- Decommissioning of wells that are no longer required (must be done properly to prevent cross-contamination between aquifers)

Reference: NEPM 2013 Schedule A (long-term monitoring guidance); state-specific site audit guidelines; Environmental Management Plan requirements
Applies to: Sites where residual contamination remains after remediation, subject to ongoing management conditions
Related standards: AS/NZS 5667.1 (water sampling); respective EPA monitoring guidelines

Common pitfalls:
- Not budgeting for long-term monitoring costs at the project feasibility stage, leading to unfunded obligations that fall on the property owner
- Allowing monitoring wells to fall into disrepair, compromising the quality of monitoring data and potentially triggering regulatory action
- Not transferring monitoring obligations to new owners when the property is sold, leaving the obligations unfulfilled and potentially resulting in regulatory non-compliance
- Treating the monitoring program as purely administrative without analysing trends in the data — monitoring should actively assess whether contamination is stable, declining, or worsening
- Not maintaining communication with the site auditor (if the audit statement includes conditions), which can lead to compliance issues when the auditor reviews the monitoring data

## Risk Assessment Approaches for Remediation Projects

Risk assessment in the remediation context has two distinct applications: environmental risk assessment (assessing the health and ecological risks posed by contamination) and project risk assessment (assessing the commercial and delivery risks of the remediation project). Both are critical for informed decision-making.

**Environmental risk assessment:**

The NEPM Tier 2 and Tier 3 risk assessment framework is applied when contamination exceeds the Tier 1 screening levels but may not pose an actual risk given site-specific conditions. Key elements include:

- **Exposure assessment:** Identification and quantification of exposure pathways — how could a person or ecological receptor come into contact with the contamination? Pathways include ingestion of soil, dermal contact with soil, inhalation of dust or vapour, ingestion of groundwater, and consumption of produce grown in contaminated soil.
- **Toxicity assessment:** Determination of the health effects associated with each contaminant at the concentrations detected. This draws on Australian and international toxicological data.
- **Risk characterisation:** Combining exposure and toxicity data to determine whether the contamination poses an unacceptable risk. This typically involves calculating a hazard quotient (HQ) or incremental lifetime cancer risk (ILCR) and comparing against acceptable thresholds (HQ <1; ILCR <10⁻⁵ for individual contaminants, <10⁻⁴ for cumulative).

A site-specific risk assessment may demonstrate that contamination above the generic screening levels does not pose an unacceptable risk at the particular site, potentially avoiding or reducing the scope of remediation.

**Project risk assessment:**

For the project manager and quantity surveyor, the key project risks on remediation projects include:

- **Volume risk:** The actual volume of contaminated material exceeds the estimate (most common and highest-impact risk)
- **Classification risk:** Excavated material is classified at a higher waste category than expected, increasing disposal costs
- **Groundwater risk:** Groundwater contamination is more extensive or concentrated than the investigation indicated
- **Program risk:** Remediation takes longer than planned due to unexpected contamination, weather delays, or regulatory requirements
- **Regulatory risk:** Changes in environmental criteria or regulatory requirements during the remediation program
- **Stakeholder risk:** Community opposition, adjacent property owner disputes, or political sensitivity
- **Technology risk:** The selected remediation technology does not perform as expected

Project risk assessment should use the same risk register methodology applied to standard construction projects (AS/NZS ISO 31000:2018), with probability and consequence ratings for each identified risk and a mitigation strategy. The key difference is the weighting of environmental and regulatory risks, which are more prominent than on standard construction projects.

Reference: NEPM 2013, Schedule B4 — Guideline on site-specific health risk assessment; AS/NZS ISO 31000:2018 (Risk management guidelines); enHealth Environmental Health Risk Assessment guidelines
Applies to: All contaminated site assessments and remediation projects
Related standards: NEPM 2013 Schedule B4 and Schedule B6 (risk-based guidance)

Common pitfalls:
- Relying on a site-specific risk assessment to avoid remediation without considering the practical and reputational implications — a technically defensible risk assessment showing acceptable risk may still be unacceptable to the community, the lender, or the insurer
- Not maintaining the project risk register during remediation, allowing emerging risks to go unmanaged
- Setting the project budget based on the most likely scenario without adequately provisioning for the downside (P80 or P90) scenario
- Failing to conduct a pre-remediation risk workshop with the project team, environmental consultant, and remediation contractor to identify and price all significant risks

## Remediation in the Construction Program

Integrating remediation works into the overall construction program requires careful planning because remediation activities have characteristics that differ from standard construction:

**Program considerations:**

- **Remediation typically precedes construction:** The site must be validated as suitable before building construction can commence. This means remediation sits on the critical path.
- **Weather sensitivity:** Remediation works involving excavation, soil treatment, and earthworks are highly weather-sensitive. Wet weather can halt excavation, saturate treated soil stockpiles, and generate contaminated runoff.
- **Regulatory hold points:** Site audit reviews, EPA approvals, and validation assessments create mandatory hold points in the program that cannot be accelerated.
- **Phased remediation:** On large sites, it may be possible to phase remediation and construction — remediate and validate one area while continuing investigation or remediation on another. This requires careful planning and physical separation (temporary fencing, silt fences, air monitoring) between the remediated and unremediated zones.
- **Temporary works:** Remediation may require temporary works (dewatering systems, groundwater treatment plants, soil treatment areas, stockpile containment) that occupy space needed for subsequent construction activities. The transition from remediation to construction must be planned to allow demobilisation of remediation infrastructure.

**Typical remediation program durations:**

- Minor soil remediation (localised excavation and validation): 2-8 weeks
- Moderate soil remediation (UST removal, broader excavation): 2-6 months
- Major soil remediation (large-scale excavation and import): 6-18 months
- Groundwater remediation (pump and treat or in-situ): 2-10+ years (often runs in parallel with construction and occupation)
- Site audit process (from submission of validation report to receipt of audit statement): 2-6 months

**Early works packages:**

Remediation is often procured as an early works package, separate from the main construction contract. This has several advantages:
- Allows remediation to start before the main construction contract is awarded
- Isolates the remediation cost risk from the main construction contract
- Enables specialist remediation contractors to be engaged directly
- Provides a clear milestone (validation and audit) before the main construction contract commences

Reference: Project programming best practices; NSW EPA guidelines on remediation project planning
Applies to: All construction projects that include remediation as part of the development program
Related standards: AS 4000-1997 (for early works contract arrangements)

Common pitfalls:
- Not including remediation in the overall project program, treating it as a pre-project activity rather than a programmed work package with defined duration and dependencies
- Underestimating the duration of the site audit process, which creates a gap between completing remediation and commencing construction
- Failing to plan for the transition between remediation and construction phases, leading to clashes over site access, laydown areas, and temporary infrastructure
- Not programming weather contingency for remediation earthworks, which are more sensitive to weather disruptions than most construction activities
- Assuming remediation and construction can proceed simultaneously without physical separation and environmental controls between the two work zones
