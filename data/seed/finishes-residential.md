# Trade seed — Finishes (residential)

This seed provides residential-grade finishes context for Class 1a buildings. It covers external envelope, roofing, internal linings, wet area waterproofing, tiling, joinery, glazing, and flooring. It gives the agent and project lead sufficient literacy to scope finishes work, identify inspection gates, recognise common defects, and flag when specialist input is required.

Load this seed task-loaded when a role × archetype task involves finishes scope of works, specification items, defect identification, or PC inspection preparation.

---

## External envelope

### Brick veneer

Brick veneer is the dominant external cladding in suburban NSW and VIC. A single leaf of clay or concrete masonry is tied to a timber or steel stud frame with wall ties; the frame carries all structural load.

**Key elements:**
- **Brick ties:** Galvanised or stainless steel ties at specified centres (typically 600 mm vertical, 900 mm horizontal). Missing or incorrectly spaced ties are a structural defect.
- **Weepholes:** Openings at the base of the brick skin and above lintels to drain moisture that penetrates the cavity. Must not be blocked by mortar.
- **Cavity:** Minimum 25 mm clear cavity between brick and frame. Controls moisture and provides thermal buffer.
- **DPC (damp-proof course):** Horizontal DPC at base of brick wall above slab edge level, and above and below window openings. Prevents rising damp.
- **Lintels:** Steel or reinforced masonry lintels over all openings. Size and bearing length to structural engineer's specification.
- **Joints and movement:** Vertical expansion joints at maximum 6–8 m centres (clay brick) and at changes of direction. Missing joints cause cracking.

**Common defects:**
- Hollow or missing mortar joints (pointing defects) visible on inspection
- Efflorescence (white salt deposits) indicating moisture movement — usually cosmetic but can indicate DPC failure
- Brick cracks at corners, openings, or mid-span — typically differential movement; may be structural if wide (> 3 mm) or stepped

**Inspection gate:** Brickwork is inspected before external render or coating is applied. Evidence: builder's quality inspection record; engineer review if structural cracks present.

### Rendered masonry and texture coat

Render is a cement-sand or polymer-modified coating applied over masonry (AAC/Hebel, concrete block, or over-brick). Texture coat is an acrylic-based finish coat applied over a render base.

**Key risks:**
- Cracking at control joints: joints must be provided and matched through the render coat
- Render over AAC: AAC (autoclaved aerated concrete) blocks require a compatible render system (not dense cement render) and alkali-resistant mesh at joints
- Hollow areas: render that has debonded from the substrate sounds hollow when tapped — a defect requiring investigation

### Fibre cement sheet (FC sheet)

FC sheet (e.g. James Hardie Hardiflex, Scyon, Villaboard) is used for cladding, eaves lining, and wet area substrates. External cladding grades (e.g. Hardie Panel, Scyon Axon) are designed for weatherboard-profile or sheet cladding applications.

**Key compliance:**
- Sheet must be installed as per manufacturer's specifications (fixing centres, gap tolerances, joint treatment)
- All cut edges must be sealed
- External sheet requires an approved paint system — unpainted FC sheet degrades
- Not suitable for direct ground contact

### Weatherboard / timber cladding

Horizontal timber weatherboards (hardwood or softwood) or fibre cement weatherboard profiles. Requires:
- Treated pine or hardwood for external exposure (H3 or H4 hazard class per AS 1604)
- Back-primed before installation
- Clearance from ground and from roof cladding per manufacturer
- Overlap dimensions maintained consistently

## Roofing

### Roof tile systems

**Terracotta tiles:** Heavy (approx. 45–55 kg/m²), durable, attractive. The structural engineer must confirm the roof frame is designed for the tile load — substituting tiles for metal after frame approval requires engineer review.

**Concrete tiles:** Slightly heavier than terracotta, similar structural implications. Standard in many NSW residential projects.

**Key elements:**
- **Sarking:** Reflective foil sarking is required under tiles in most climate zones (NCC energy requirements; also acts as a secondary moisture barrier). Must be lapped and taped.
- **Battens:** Tiling battens spaced per tile manufacturer's specification; must be fixed to each rafter.
- **Fixings:** Ridge tiles, hip tiles, and valley tiles must be mechanically fixed (not just mortar-bedded) per AS 2050.
- **Ridge capping:** Pointed or dry-fixed. Dry-fixed (mechanically fixed with foam closures) preferred — mortar-bedded ridges are a common maintenance issue.
- **Flashings:** Lead or aluminium flashings at wall-to-roof junctions, penetrations (chimneys, vents, skylights), and abutments. Must be fully integrated before tiles laid.
- **Valley gutters:** Pressed metal (zincalume or copper) valley gutters at internal roof angles. Critically important — blocked or damaged valleys are a major leak source.

### Metal sheet roofing (Colorbond / Zincalume)

Corrugated or trapezoidal profile steel sheet (Colorbond steel by BlueScope, or Zincalume uncoated). Significantly lighter than tiles (approx. 5–8 kg/m²).

**Key elements:**
- **Minimum pitch:** Each profile has a minimum installation pitch. Below minimum pitch, capillary action causes water ingress. Always check manufacturer's spec for the profile selected.
- **Fixing:** Concealed or exposed fasteners. Exposed fasteners have neoprene washers that degrade over time — an ongoing maintenance item.
- **Lap direction:** Sheets must lap in the direction of water flow.
- **Thermal expansion:** Metal roofing expands and contracts significantly. Fixings must allow for movement; over-tightening causes oil-canning (surface distortion).
- **Flashings:** Purpose-designed metal flashings for all penetrations and abutments, matching the roofing profile.

### Flat roofs and membrane systems

Flat and low-pitch roofs (< 5°) require a waterproof membrane. Systems include:
- **TPO / PVC membrane:** Thermoplastic sheets, heat-welded seams
- **Modified bitumen (torch-on):** Built-up membrane layers; torch applied
- **Liquid-applied membranes:** Polyurethane or acrylic, brush/roller applied; useful for complex shapes

All membrane systems require:
- Correct substrate (dry, no moisture, correctly primed)
- Correct lap and seal dimensions at seams
- Perimeter edge detail and overflow drainage
- Maintenance access for inspection

Flat roofs are a higher defect risk than pitched roofs. Inspection before covering is critical.

## Internal linings

### Plasterboard (gypsum board)

Standard internal wall and ceiling lining in residential construction. Supplied in 10 mm, 13 mm, and 16 mm thicknesses; moisture-resistant (MR), fire-rated (FR), and abuse-resistant grades.

**Key installation points:**
- **Wet areas:** MR grade required in bathrooms, laundries, and kitchen splashback areas — standard plasterboard will delaminate if wetted
- **Fixing centres:** Standard 300 mm centres on walls, 450 mm (sometimes 300 mm) on ceilings. Inadequate fixing causes sagging, nail popping, and joint cracking.
- **Joints:** Recessed-edge boards enable flush taping and jointing. Set joint must be applied in three coats (base, fill, finish) and allowed to cure between coats.
- **Expansion joints:** Required in large continuous areas (> 9 m on walls, > 12 m on ceilings) to allow movement.
- **Wet area waterproofing:** Plasterboard in wet areas is a substrate for waterproofing membranes and tiling — the waterproofing layer, not the board, provides the water barrier. Plasterboard must not be the final surface in shower enclosures or bathtub surrounds.

**Common defects:**
- Cracking at ceiling–wall junctions (typically seasonal movement; may indicate structural movement if recurring)
- Sagging ceilings (inadequate fixing, moisture ingress, or excessive joist span)
- Joint ridging (under-set, over-set, or inadequate curing between coats)
- Nail/screw popping (insufficient fixing depth or timber shrinkage)

## Wet area waterproofing

### Regulatory requirement

NCC Volume Two (Housing Provisions) and AS 3740 (Waterproofing of domestic wet areas) govern waterproofing in bathrooms, ensuites, laundries, and balconies.

**AS 3740 classification:**
- **Class 1:** Shower recesses and floor areas within shower screens
- **Class 2:** Floor areas outside Class 1 within the wet area
- **Class 3:** Walls

Required membrane height:
- Shower recess walls: minimum 1800 mm from floor (or to the underside of any ceiling lining if lower)
- Hob-less shower floor: extends to shower screen line and at least 150 mm beyond
- Balcony / deck: full floor area plus 100 mm up all walls

### Application requirements

- Substrate must be clean, sound, and free of movement before membrane application
- Two-coat minimum for sheet membranes; flood-coat minimum for liquid-applied systems
- Curing period must be observed before tiling (typically 24–48 hours minimum, manufacturer-specific)
- Corners and junctions require fabric reinforcing strip embedded in the membrane

### Inspection gate

Waterproofing is a mandatory hold point under NCC/BCA. In NSW, the certifier must inspect and approve the membrane before tiling proceeds. Evidence: certifier's waterproofing inspection certificate.

**This is a non-negotiable gate.** The agent must not allow tiling to be scheduled before waterproofing inspection is confirmed. Tiling over uninspected waterproofing is one of the most common defect-cost exposures in residential construction.

## Tiling

### Substrate preparation

Tiling to walls must go over a sound, flat substrate:
- **Walls:** MR plasterboard, FC sheet (Villaboard or equivalent), or masonry
- **Floors:** Concrete slab or FC sheet floor cassette. Particleboard (standard timber sheet flooring) is not a suitable direct tiling substrate without a membrane — it expands with moisture.

Flatness tolerance: AS 3958.1 (Guide to installation of ceramic tiles) — maximum deviation of 3 mm under a 3 m straightedge for standard tile formats; tighter tolerances for large-format tiles.

### Adhesive and grout selection

- **Adhesive class** (AS ISO 13007): C1 (standard cementitious), C2 (improved cementitious, required for large-format tiles and external use), R (rapid setting), S (flexible — required for external, heated substrates, and over membranes)
- **Grout:** Unsanded grout for joints < 3 mm; sanded grout for joints ≥ 3 mm. Epoxy grout for commercial-grade applications or areas requiring chemical resistance.

### Expansion and movement joints

AS 3958 requires movement joints at:
- Perimeters (floor-to-wall junction)
- Centres: every 4.5 m in each direction for internal floor tiles; every 3 m for external tiles
- At all structural movement joints in the substrate

Missing movement joints are the primary cause of cracked floor tiles.

### Common defects

- **Hollow tiles:** Loose or debonded tiles that sound hollow when tapped — indicate inadequate adhesive coverage (< 85% contact area required)
- **Lippage:** Height variation between adjacent tile edges — caused by substrate unevenness, inconsistent joint widths, or tile warpage; visible and tactile defect
- **Cracked tiles:** Caused by substrate movement, inadequate adhesive, or impact. Cracking in a pattern indicates substrate movement rather than installation defect.
- **Grout cracking:** In movement joint locations, indicates joints should be raked out and replaced with sealant, not grout

## Joinery

### Kitchen cabinetry

Residential kitchen joinery is typically flat-pack (IKEA-style) or custom-made. Key specification items:
- **Carcase material:** 16 mm or 18 mm melamine-faced particleboard (MFC) — moisture resistant grade (MR-E0 or MR-E1 formaldehyde emission) for under-sink and wet-zone applications
- **Door material:** MFC, polyurethane (2-pack PU), vinyl-wrapped, or timber veneer
- **Hardware:** Concealed hinge specification (opening angle, soft-close), drawer runner load rating, and undermount runners for premium applications
- **Benchtop:** Laminate, stone (engineered quartz or natural stone), timber, or solid surface. Stone bench tops are heavy — confirm cabinet support and installation clearances

**Inspection:** Kitchen cabinetry is inspected as part of the fixing inspection. Evidence of square, level installation, correct door alignment, and undamaged benchtop is required before PC.

### Wardrobes and built-in cabinetry

Similar carcase construction to kitchens. Sliding door systems require correct track installation and door alignment. Walk-in robes require adequate ventilation to prevent moisture build-up.

### Window and door joinery (aluminium and timber)

Aluminium windows and doors dominate new residential construction. Key items:
- **Frame type:** Thermally broken vs standard aluminium extrusion — thermally broken reduces condensation and heat loss in cold climates
- **Glazing specification:** See Glazing section below
- **Flashing and sealing:** Critical interface item; see `trade-interfaces-coordination-guide.md`
- **Compliance with wind loading:** Window selection must match the building's wind classification (N or C class)

## Glazing

### Safety glazing requirements

NCC Volume Two and AS 1288 (Glass in buildings) require safety glazing in:
- All glazed panels within 300 mm of a door opening
- Sidelights adjacent to doors
- Low-level glazing (bottom edge < 500 mm from floor)
- Wet area glazing (shower screens, bath screens, pool fencing)
- Balustrades and frameless glass panels

Safety glazing is either **toughened (tempered) glass** (AS/NZS 2208) or **laminated glass** (AS 1288). Etched or frosted finishes do not change the safety classification — the glass must still be toughened or laminated to be a safety glass.

**Common defect:** Standard annealed glass installed in safety glass locations. This is a serious NCC non-compliance. The agent must flag unspecified glazing in safety locations as requiring verification.

### Double glazing and energy performance

Double-glazed units (IGU — insulated glass units) are increasingly specified for energy performance and acoustic attenuation. BASIX may drive IGU selection in some climate zones. Key specification items:
- Gap width (typically 12 mm or 16 mm argon-filled)
- Low-E coating (reduces radiant heat transfer)
- WERS (Window Energy Rating Scheme) star rating

## Flooring

### Timber flooring (solid and engineered)

**Solid hardwood:** 19 mm thick, nailed or glued to timber subfloor. Not suitable for direct installation over concrete slab without a moisture barrier and substrate preparation. Subject to expansion and contraction with humidity — acclimatisation period (typically 7–14 days on site) required before laying.

**Engineered timber:** Plywood core with hardwood veneer face (typically 3–6 mm face). More dimensionally stable than solid hardwood; can be floated, glued, or nailed. Some engineered products are suitable for installation over in-slab heating. Always confirm manufacturer's specifications for substrate type.

**Common defects:**
- Gapping between boards (excessive humidity loss after laying)
- Cupping or crowning (moisture differential between top and bottom face — inadequate moisture barrier or wet slab)
- Squeaking (inadequate fixing, or movement of boards over substrate)

### Luxury vinyl plank / tile (LVP / LVT)

Click-lock or glue-down vinyl products. Highly moisture resistant; suitable for kitchens, bathrooms, and laundries in residential. Key specification items:
- Wear layer thickness: ≥ 0.3 mm for residential, ≥ 0.5 mm for high-traffic residential
- Total thickness: affects height differential at door thresholds and at transitions to other floor materials
- Installation method: floating (click-lock) or glue-down. Floating requires underlay and is not suitable for wet areas at floor penetrations unless glue-down at wet zones.

### Carpet

Soft floor covering for bedrooms and living areas. Key specification items:
- **Pile type:** Loop pile (Berber), cut pile, cut-and-loop
- **Fibre:** Wool (premium, resilient), nylon (durable), polyester (budget), polypropylene (entry-level)
- **Underlay:** Foam, felt, or rubber. Thickness affects feel and durability. Minimum 6 mm for residential.

Carpet must not be installed until painting is complete and wet trades have finished — moisture and foot traffic damage freshly installed carpet.

### Polished concrete

Grinding and polishing of the structural slab. Requires:
- Slab poured to a higher quality specification (surface flatness, aggregate control, reduced cracking)
- Protection during construction (difficult to repair once damaged)
- Sealing and maintenance program

Only viable if specified at the design stage — cannot be retrofitted to a standard slab finish without significant grinding.

## Common defects at key trade interfaces

- **Hollow tiles** (tiler-to-waterproofer interface): waterproofing membrane not fully cured before tiling; adhesive not spread correctly over membrane
- **Plasterboard joint cracking** at ceiling–wall junction: plasterboard fixed before framing has dried out; no control joint provided
- **Paint adhesion failure on new plaster**: painting commenced before plaster is fully cured and dry (minimum 4 weeks for base set, longer for thick coats); incompatible primer used
- **Render cracking at windows**: window frame not sealed before render; no backing rod at render-to-frame joint
- **Floor squeaking** under carpet: particleboard subfloor sheets not glued and screwed; boards have swollen from moisture
- **Glazing fogging** (IGU seal failure): typically a manufacturing defect or installation damage; argon gas escapes and moisture enters the sealed unit

## Low-confidence flags

- **Brick crack assessment:** Wide (> 3 mm), stepped, or recurring cracks in brickwork may indicate structural movement rather than cosmetic cracking. Do not assess as cosmetic — route to a structural engineer or building inspector.
- **Heritage / face-brick matching:** Matching existing brick types, mortar colour, and bond pattern on renovation projects is specialist work. Do not advise on specific heritage brick sources or mortars from general knowledge.
- **Tile selection for wet areas over in-slab heating:** Not all tiles or adhesives are suitable for heated substrates. Confirm system compatibility before specifying.
- **Acoustic requirements (multi-dwelling):** Party wall and party floor acoustic performance requirements (NCC Specification 43) are not covered in this seed — flag for multi-dwelling projects and refer to an acoustic engineer.
- **External timber decking:** Timber species selection for external decking (durability class, treatability, slip resistance) is not covered in detail. Refer to AS 1604 and manufacturer specifications.
